The Two Wheels

Sadhan Chakra & Jnana Chakra

These two wheels turn together — one outward, one inward. Sadhana without Jnana becomes mechanical ritual. Jnana without Sadhana becomes empty intellectualism. The roaring path requires both wheels in motion.

Sadhan Chakra

The Wheel of Practice

The outer and inner practices that transform the practitioner from a seeker into a knower. Sadhana is not performance for reward; it is the natural expression of one who has heard the teaching and cannot rest until it is realized.

  • Morning Sitting (Pratah Upasana): Daily meditation at dawn, before the mind accumulates the day's dust. Sit in silence, observe the breath, let thoughts arise and pass without engagement.
  • Simplified Pancha-Devata Puja: Worship of the five forms of Brahman — Surya (illumination), Ganesha (removal of obstacles), Vishnu (preservation of dharma), Shiva (transformation), Devi (the dynamic power). No initiation required; the form is a symbol, the understanding is the substance.
  • Mantra Japa: Repetition of the Mahavakya or a chosen mantra with attention and devotion. The mantra is not magic; it is a vehicle for focusing the mind on the Self.
  • Self-Inquiry (Atma-Vichara): The question "Who am I?" asked not as philosophy but as a living investigation. Trace every thought, every feeling, every sensation back to its source — the witness that is never absent.
  • Silence (Mauna): Periodic observance of silence — not merely of speech, but of the mind's chatter. In silence, the roaring of the Self becomes audible.
  • Seva (Service): The primary external sadhana. Unconditional service to the world through one's genuine nature, skill, and life-station. Not transactional charity, but Brahman serving Brahman through this particular form.
  • Study (Svadhyaya): Daily engagement with the primary texts — not as academic exercise, but as listening to the guru's voice across time.
  • Satsanga: Association with those who seek the truth. The circle of practitioners who question, support, and challenge one another to remain true to the path.

Jnana Chakra

The Wheel of Knowledge

The systematic unfolding of understanding that transforms information into recognition. Jnana is not accumulation of facts; it is the removal of ignorance that reveals what was always true. The wheel turns through three stages: hearing (sravana), reflection (manana), and deep meditation (nididhyasana).

  • Sravana (Hearing): Listening to the teaching with an open mind and sincere heart. Not merely hearing words, but allowing the meaning to penetrate. The texts — Tattvabodha, Upadesha Sahasri, Sarvavedantasiddhantasara Sangraha — are the guru's voice.
  • Manana (Reflection): Systematic reflection on what has been heard. Using Aanvikshiki — perception, inference, analogy, and reliable testimony — to resolve doubts and confirm understanding. Question everything, including the teaching itself, until it stands on its own.
  • Nididhyasana (Deep Meditation): Sustained contemplation on the truth of the teaching until it becomes direct experience. Not thinking about the Self, but abiding as the Self. The difference between knowing about water and drinking it.
  • Discrimination (Viveka): The constant discrimination between the real (Sat) and the unreal (Asat). The Self is real; the body, mind, and world are appearances upon it. This discrimination is the sword that cuts the knot of ignorance.
  • Detachment (Vairagya): Dispassion toward the results of actions and the objects of the senses. Not suppression or denial, but the natural falling away of interest in what cannot satisfy, once the real has been tasted.
  • The Six Treasures (Shat-Sampat): Sama (calmness), Dama (self-control), Uparati (withdrawal), Titiksha (forbearance), Shraddha (faith in the teaching), Samadhana (concentration). These are the soil in which the seed of knowledge grows.
  • Mumukshutva (Longing for Liberation): The burning desire for freedom that drives the seeker through every obstacle. Not a whim or passing interest, but the central fire around which all life revolves.
  • Direct Recognition (Aparoksha-Anubhuti): The culmination — not a new experience, but the recognition that the Self was never absent, never bound, never other than what it is. The roaring silence that remains when all seeking ceases.
GARJANAA

The two wheels turn together. Practice and knowledge, hand in hand, toward the ever-free Self.
