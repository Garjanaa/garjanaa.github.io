Advaita Vedanta

The Philosophy of Non‑Duality

Our path rests on the understanding that consciousness is not divided: that the individual self (jīva) and the universal ground (Brahman) are not two. This is the core of Advaita Vedānta.

The Mahāvākyas

The Upaniṣads distill this truth into four great sayings: Ahaṃ Brahmāsmi (I am Brahman), Tat Tvam Asi (That thou art), Prajñānaṃ Brahma (Consciousness is Brahman), and Ayam Ātmā Brahma (This self is Brahman). Each is a complete statement of the non‑dual recognition, not a doctrine to be memorized but a declaration to be investigated.

Three Orders of Reality

Advaita distinguishes the absolute (Paramārthika), the conventional (Vyāvahārika), and the merely apparent (Prātibhāsika). This framework does real work: it lets us take the world seriously, act ethically, and care for our relationships, while recognizing that the conventional order is not the final word on what we are. Liberation is not escape from the world but correct understanding of it.

Four Paths to Liberation

We integrate Jñāna (knowledge), Bhakti (devotion), Karma (selfless action), and Rāja (meditation), not as competitors but as convergent streams. A practitioner draws from all four in the proportion that suits their own temperament, trusting that followed honestly, each path meets the others at the same recognition.

The Values We Cultivate

The Daivī Sampada (divine qualities) from the Bhagavad Gītā: fearlessness, purity, steadiness, compassion, non‑harm, truthfulness, freedom from anger, generosity, gentleness, and freedom from envy and excessive pride. These are not virtues to perform. They are what a mind naturally looks like once it is no longer run by anxiety and the need to protect a separate, limited self.

We also name the Āsurī Sampada (the shadow‑qualities: pride disguised as virtue, arrogance, anger, cruelty, willful ignorance) clearly and without flinching, because a community that cannot name its own shadow cannot guard against it. The Garjanaa Codes of Conduct address both in detail, with specific antidotes and community‑level safeguards.

Politically Aligned to the Divine Qualities

This circle does not affiliate with political parties or movements. It aligns itself, in every decision about how to organize, speak, and act, with the Daivī Sampada as described in the Gītā: truthfulness, fearlessness, compassion, non‑harm, and freedom from greed. Where political questions touch these values directly, the values come first. The Garjanaa Smriti’s principles of transparency, voluntary participation, protection of the vulnerable, and accountability to texts reflect this alignment concretely.

Self‑Growth as the Purpose

Every gathering, every study session, every shared practice in this circle has one function: to help each practitioner see more clearly, act more rightly, and rest more fully in the recognition of their own true nature. Community building is the vehicle. Self‑growth, in the deepest sense the tradition names, is the destination.
