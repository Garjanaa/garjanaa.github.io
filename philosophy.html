Four Pillars

Philosophy and Values

I. Advaita Vedanta: The Core Philosophy

Our path rests on the understanding that consciousness is not divided: that the individual self (jiva) and the universal ground (Brahman) are not two. This is the core of Advaita Vedanta, the non-dual school of the Upanishads, systematized by Adi Shankaracharya, transmitted by Ramana Maharshi, and alive in every practitioner who seriously asks the question "Who am I?"

Advaita does not ask for belief. It asks for investigation. The claim is not a doctrine to accept; it is a description of what direct, honest self-inquiry reveals. The method is the Mahavakyas, the great sayings of the Upanishads, and the three-stage process of shravana (careful study), manana (rigorous reflection), and nididhyasana (sustained meditative dwelling).

The Mahavakyas

Four sentences from the Upanishads state the identity of the individual self and ultimate reality directly. Prajnanam Brahma (Consciousness is Brahman, Rig Veda). Aham Brahmasmi (I am Brahman, Yajur Veda). Tat Tvam Asi (That thou art, Sama Veda). Ayam Atma Brahma (This Self is Brahman, Atharva Veda). Each is a complete statement of the non-dual recognition, not a doctrine to memorize but a declaration to investigate.

Three Orders of Reality

Advaita distinguishes three levels: the absolute (Paramarthika), the conventional (Vyavaharika), and the merely apparent (Pratibhasika). This framework does real work. It lets us take the world seriously, act ethically, engage rigorously with empirical questions, and care fully for our relationships, while recognizing that the conventional order is not the final word on what we are. Liberation is not escape from the world but correct understanding of it.

II. Pancha-devata: Five Symbols of One Brahman

The Agamic tradition offers the Pancha-devata, five forms through which the one undivided Brahman is approached in ritual and devotion. These are not five separate gods. They are five doorways into the same recognition, each illuminating a different aspect of the one reality. Approach any one of them sincerely and you arrive at the same place.

🐘 Ganesha Intelligence Brahman as the remover of obstacles. The principle of discriminating intelligence that clears the way for all genuine inquiry. The one who is invoked at every beginning.
☀ Surya Illumination Brahman as self-luminous awareness, the visible form of the light by which everything else is known. The Gayatri is addressed to this illumination: may it illuminate our understanding.
🌊 Vishnu Sustenance Brahman as the ground that pervades and sustains all existence. The blue of infinite sky and deep ocean. The Dharma that holds the world together across time.
🌑 Shiva Transcendence Brahman as the pure witness, the one who dissolves ego and returns everything to its source. The ash on the forehead: all things return to what they always were. The third eye of direct perception.
🔥 Devi / Shakti Creative Power Brahman as the dynamic creative power, Maya as the Divine Mother who both veils and reveals. Durga removes what blocks. Lakshmi establishes right relationship. Saraswati flows as wisdom.

These five are approached through the simplified daily puja described in the Practices section. No human guru is required. The forms themselves are the teacher. Understanding their symbolic meaning is the essential preparation; the puja is the ongoing practice of that understanding.

III. Bhakti: Love in Advaitic Flavor

Bhakti is not the consolation path for those who cannot do jnana. It is a complete path that arrives at the identical recognition. The greatest bhaktas, Ramakrishna, Mirabai, Andal, Tukaram, arrived at non-dual recognition through the most intense personal love. The Beloved and the lover turned out to be not two.

In the Advaitic understanding of bhakti, love is the felt dimension of non-dual recognition. The devotee who surrenders completely, holding nothing back, finds that the one who was surrendering dissolves into what was being surrendered to. The drop does not disappear in the ocean. It becomes the ocean. This is not loss; it is the discovery that the drop was always the ocean, seen from a limited angle.

The three modes of devotion, dasyabhava (the servant's love), sakhyabhava (the friend's love), and madhurabhava (the lover's love), all lead to the same dissolution when followed to their natural depth. Bhakti toward the Pancha-devata is celebrated here not as a lower practice to be eventually replaced, but as a genuine and complete path in its own right. Shankara himself wrote devotional hymns of great beauty alongside his most rigorous philosophical works, and the tradition sees no contradiction.

In practice: kirtan, puja, and devotional prayer are integral to this community's life. Love is a valid instrument of inquiry. What it reveals, when followed all the way, is what jnana also reveals: the Self alone is.

IV. Aanvikshiki: The Science of Inquiry

Aanvikshiki is an ancient Indian term for the science of critical inquiry and logical investigation. Kautilya's Arthashastra calls it "the lamp of all sciences, the resource for all actions, and the support of all dharmas." It encompasses the Nyaya school's epistemology and the Vaisheshika school's natural philosophy, and it is the framework this community uses for engaging the empirical world honestly.

Nyaya: The Logic of Valid Knowledge

Nyaya, founded by Gautama in the Nyaya Sutras, provides four pramanas (valid sources of knowledge) for the empirical world: pratyaksha (direct perception), anumana (inference through sound reasoning), upamana (analogy and comparison), and shabda (reliable testimony from competent authority). The scientific method is the most rigorous modern expression of the first two. Critical thinking as a daily discipline is the practice of the third and fourth.

The Nyaya syllogism, with its five members (statement, reason, example, application, conclusion), provides the formal structure for any valid argument. The tradition's careful cataloging of fallacies (hetvabhasa) provides the tools for identifying where arguments go wrong. This is not abstract academic philosophy; it is the discipline of thinking clearly before acting and speaking.

Vaisheshika: The Categories of the Natural World

Vaisheshika, founded by Kanada in the Vaisheshika Sutras, provides a systematic categorization of the natural world through seven padarthas: substance, quality, motion, universal, particular, inherence, and non-existence. This framework maps broadly onto what the modern sciences do when they categorize, measure, and analyze natural phenomena. At the vyavaharika (empirical) level, this framework is fully valid and fully applicable.

The Relationship Between Aanvikshiki and Vedanta

These two systems operate at different levels and do not conflict. Aanvikshiki and Nyaya-Vaisheshika operate at the vyavaharika (empirical) level, providing precision tools for engaging the conventional world honestly. Advaita Vedanta addresses the paramarthika (ultimate) level: what is real when all conventional framing is set aside. Using Nyaya to analyze an empirical claim and Vedanta to understand the nature of the analyzer are not in tension. They address different questions at different levels, and the tradition has always known this.

In practice: this community applies critical thinking, sound inference, and empirical evidence to all claims about the conventional world. No authority, including ancient ones, escapes this standard in the empirical domain. Simultaneously, the tools of conventional analysis are recognized as not the final word on the nature of consciousness itself, which is Vedanta's domain.

V. The Values We Cultivate

The Daivi Sampada from the Bhagavad Gita describes the natural character of a mind that is genuinely aligning itself with truth: fearlessness, purity of mind, steadiness in the practice of wisdom, generosity, sense mastery, a spirit of selfless offering, self-study, disciplined austerity, and straightforwardness. Non-harming in thought, word, and deed. Truthfulness. Freedom from anger. Renunciation of ego-ownership over actions and their fruits. Freedom from fault-finding, malice, and excessive pride. Peacefulness. Compassion toward all beings. Freedom from covetousness. Gentleness. Appropriate restraint. Steadiness. Vital inner brightness arising from integrity. Forgiveness. Patient endurance. Cleanliness of body and mind.

We also name the shadow qualities clearly: pride disguised as virtue, arrogance, harshness, anger, willful ignorance, hypocrisy, and an insatiable reckless craving for sensory gratification. A community that cannot name its own shadow cannot guard against it.

Politically Aligned to the Divine Qualities

This circle does not affiliate with political parties or movements. In every decision about how to organize, speak, and act, it aligns itself with the Daivi Sampada: truthfulness, fearlessness, compassion, non-harming, and freedom from greed. Where political questions directly touch these values, the values come first. The community's principles of transparency, voluntary participation, protection of the vulnerable, and accountability to the texts reflect this alignment concretely.
