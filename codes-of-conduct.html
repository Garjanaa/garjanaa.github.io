GARJANAA SMRITI

A Complete Manual for Living the Path, Knowledge, Union, Practice, and Conduct for the Twenty-First Century

Grounded in Advaita Vedānta, drawing on the spirit of the Āgamas and the Mahānirvāṇa Tantra, non-sectarian, non-hereditary, casteless, open to all who come sincerely.

How to Use This Smriti

This is a working manual, not a book of stories or theology alone. It tells you what to understand, if you want a map of reality; how knowledge and devotion actually work as a single path, if you want to know why we practice as we do; what to do each day, if you want a daily discipline; and how to mark the great turnings of a human life, birth, coming of age, marriage, old age, death, and how to live together as a community without falling into the traps that corrupt spiritual communities.

Nothing here is compulsory. Nothing requires a hereditary priest, a secret initiation, or a living guru to perform. Adapt it, shorten it, translate it into your own words. The form is a container; sincerity is what fills it.

The Smriti has four Khandas (parts), plus a short closing:

  • Vidyā Khaṇḍa, The Part on Knowledge. What is real, and about whom. Advaita Vedānta in outline, and each of the five streams (Pañcadevatā) through which this community approaches the one Brahman.
  • Yoga Khaṇḍa, The Part on Union. How knowledge and devotion are, at the root, one path wearing two faces, and what it means to practice devotion as the Self, rather than devotion aimed at something external to it.
  • Kriyā Khaṇḍa, The Part on Practice. The specific daily disciplines: posture, breath, consecration of the body, mantra, self-inquiry, silence, study, community, service, and ordinary life.
  • Ācāra Khaṇḍa, The Part on Conduct. Daily conduct, worldly wisdom (Nīti) for trust, speech, money, and alliance, the rites of passage from before birth to after death, the definition and practice of marriage, and how the community organizes, resolves conflict, and guards against its own corruption.
  • Closing. On amending this text, holding it lightly, and the recognition beneath all of it.

BOOK ONE, VIDYĀ KHAṆḌA

The Part on Knowledge

1.1 The One Reality

Everything in this path rests on one claim, and everything else is commentary on it: there is one reality, without a second, and that reality is what you already are. This one reality is called Brahman. It has no parts, no boundaries, no birth, and no death. It is existence itself, awareness itself, and the fullness that needs nothing added to it, Sat-Chit-Ānanda.

Everything else, every god, every ritual, every text, every practice described in this Smriti, exists only to help a human being stop mistaking themselves for something smaller than this. That is the entire purpose of the path. Rituals that do not serve this purpose, however old or beautiful, may be simplified or dropped. This is itself an old teaching, not a new one: the wise have always taught that scripture bends to serve the welfare of beings living in a particular time, not the other way around.

The three levels of reality. Advaita distinguishes three orders of "realness," a distinction that does real work:

  • Pāramārthika satya, absolute reality. Brahman alone: undivided, unchanging, never negated in any state or circumstance.
  • Vyāvahārika satya, everyday, transactional reality. The world of objects, bodies, relationships, cause and effect. Real within its own frame, and to be engaged accordingly, the table you eat at and the people you love are not illusions to be dismissed.
  • Prātibhāsika satya, merely apparent reality: a dream, a mistaken perception, a rope seen as a snake in poor light. Real only within its own appearing, dissolved the moment clear seeing arrives.

The relationship between the world and Brahman is often compared, loosely, to the relationship between a dream and the dreamer: the dream is completely real while it lasts, and nothing is lost by waking except the mistake about what was happening.

The root error. Śaṅkarācārya names this mistake adhyāsa, superimposition: mistaking the ego (body, mind, personal history) for the Ātman (pure awareness), the way a rope in dim light is mistaken for a snake. Every form of suffering the tradition names, fear of death, craving, the sense of separation from others, traces back to this one superimposition. Its resolution is not the acquisition of a new state; it is the correction of a single error, which then dissolves everything built upon it.

The great sayings (Mahāvākyas) compress this whole teaching into single lines:
- Aham Brahmāsmi, "I am Brahman" (Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad)
- Tat Tvam Asi, "That thou art" (Chāndogya Upaniṣad)
- Prajñānam Brahma, "Consciousness is Brahman" (Aitareya Upaniṣad)
- Ayam Ātmā Brahma, "This self is Brahman" (Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad)

1.2 Nirguṇa and Saguṇa: Two Ways of Approaching the One

If reality is one and formless, why bow to an image, chant a name, or light a lamp? Because the mind, before it can rest in the formless, generally needs something to love. A formless idea does not steady a distracted mind or comfort a grieving heart the way a name, a face, or a story does. So the one Brahman is approached in two ways at once:

  • Nirguṇa, without attributes: the silent awareness behind all experience, approached through inquiry, silence, and direct understanding.
  • Saguṇa, with attributes: Brahman wearing a face, a name, a story, so the heart has somewhere to place its love.

Neither is the "real" one and the other the "lesser" one. They are the same truth held at two depths. Devotion, sincerely followed, ripens into knowledge; knowledge, without devotion, tends to dry into pride. A practitioner uses both, in whatever proportion suits their own temperament, this proportion is developed further in Book Two.

1.3 The Method of This Path: No Guru Required, Everything Testable

Garjanaa has no hereditary priesthood, no single living guru, and no concept of a secret or higher initiation withheld from ordinary members.

The model of Dattātreya. Its model for learning is the sage Dattātreya, who told King Yadu that he had learned from twenty-four teachers, and named among them the earth, the wind, the sky, water, fire, the sun and moon, a pigeon, a python, the ocean, a moth drawn to a flame, a bee gathering honey from many flowers without possessiveness, a wild elephant, a deer caught by a hunter's song, a fish caught by its own greed, a courtesan who found detachment in a moment of disappointed hope, a young child at play, an unmarried woman alone in her house, and a maker of arrows so absorbed in his craft that a king's procession passed him unnoticed. None of these twenty-four was a human teacher claiming exclusive authority. Each was a fragment of ordinary experience, rightly attended to.

Following this model, the practitioner of Garjanaa:

  • learns from any source of genuine insight, texts, nature, community members, honest feedback from a friend, the discipline required by difficult circumstances, and sincere self-inquiry;
  • owes no person unquestioning obedience, a teacher's guidance is worth exactly as much as the truth it contains and the integrity visible in the teacher's own conduct, never more on account of a title or claimed lineage;
  • may, and should, seek out those with longer practice and deeper study when guidance is needed, receiving it with respect, but always testing what is received against the threefold standard below;
  • remembers Dattātreya's own teaching: a single verse, rightly understood and lived, achieves more than volumes memorized but never applied.

The Threefold Test. No teaching, ritual instruction, or claim within this community is accepted merely because someone in authority said it. Every claim is tested three ways:

  1. Śabda, does it accord with the source texts (the Gita, the Upaniṣads, the Yoga Vāsiṣṭha, the Āgamas, this Smriti itself)?
  2. Yukti, does it hold up under reason? Does it actually make sense?
  3. Anubhūti, does it hold up in direct experience, when tested honestly through practice?

A claim that fails this threefold test is not binding on any practitioner, regardless of who made it or how long it has been repeated. (This test returns throughout the Smriti, for teachings in Book Two, for inner states in Book Four, and as the final arbiter of any community dispute.)

1.4 On Birth, Caste, and Gender

This Smriti sets aside, deliberately and completely, any distinction of worth based on birth, caste, gender, or background. Spiritual capacity is demonstrated only through sincerity, conduct, and practice. No role in this community, teacher, elder, ritual officiant, is ever inherited by blood.

This is not a modern dilution of the tradition; it is consistent with the tradition's own highest teaching, that the Self is one and the same in every body, and that any distinction placing one soul above another on the basis of birth is avidyā, ignorance, dressed up as order. The great texts themselves bear this out: the Bhagavad Gītā was spoken on a battlefield to a warrior, not in a priest's study; the Rāmāyaṇa's author Vālmīki and the Mahābhārata's author Vyāsa were both born outside the conventional social hierarchy; the epics contain teachings from a hunter to a brahmin, from a counsellor to a king, from eternal children to embattled princes. Wisdom, at the tradition's most honest, has never accumulated in bloodlines.

1.5 The Five Streams (Pañcadevatā) in Detail

Garjanaa recognizes five streams through which people have approached the one Brahman for millennia, and holds all five as equally valid, equally complete, and without hierarchy over one another. No practitioner is required to follow all five. Each may find the one or two that speak most naturally to their own heart, this is called finding one's Iṣṭa Devatā, chosen form of the divine, with the help of an experienced fellow-practitioner if desired, though no formal initiation is required.

1.5.1 Śaiva, Śiva as Brahman

Śiva as Nirguṇa Brahman. At the highest level, Śiva is the attributeless reality. He is pure, self-luminous awareness, beyond name, form, and thought, the silent, still ground upon which the entire universe appears. The word Śiva means "auspicious," "the one in whom all things rest." The Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad declares that Śiva pervades all, the innermost Self of every being.

In his formless aspect, Śiva is represented by the Liṅga, an abstract, unadorned column, not a bodily symbol as sometimes misunderstood, but a representation of the formless, infinite Brahman, the axis of creation with no beginning and no end. Meditating on the Liṅga is meditating on the formless Self.

Śiva as Saguṇa Brahman. For the sake of devotees, the formless takes on form: the ash-smeared yogī with matted hair, seated in meditation on Mount Kailāsa. Every detail of this image is a teaching:

  • The third eye, the eye of wisdom (jñāna-cakṣus), the faculty of inner vision that burns away ignorance when it opens.
  • The crescent moon, mastery over the rising and falling moods of the mind, just as the moon waxes and wanes while Śiva remains unmoved.
  • The river Gaṅgā, descending through his hair, knowledge received with humility, filtered through grace before it nourishes the world; also the flow of subtle energy rising through the central channel.
  • The serpent at his neck, the base instincts and the fear of death, transmuted rather than denied; the awakened energy resting peacefully once its power is no longer a threat.
  • The trident (triśūla), mastery over the three guṇas (sattva, rajas, tamas) and the three states (waking, dream, sleep); Śiva as the witness (turīya) who stands beyond all three, cutting through the three knots of identification with body, mind, and ego.
  • The damarū (drum), the primordial sound from which language and knowledge are said to emerge; the rhythmic pulse of creation, the sound of Oṃ underlying the universe.
  • The ash on his body, the reminder that the perishable body is not the Self; when everything is burned away, what remains is the pure essence, the Ātman.
  • The tiger skin he sits upon, the ego, subdued and made into a seat for meditation rather than a master to be obeyed.
  • Nandi the bull at his feet, the devoted soul (jīva) turned toward its source, powerful yet utterly given over in service, resting in stillness before the vision of truth.

Śiva and Śakti. Śiva is never, in this tradition, separate from his Śakti, the still consciousness and the dynamic power that acts. This is expressed in the form of Ardhanārīśvara, half-male and half-female: reality is not two; it is one whole appearing as two complementary aspects, as fire and its heat cannot be divided. Some Śaiva texts describe the entire universe as the play (spanda) of Śiva's own consciousness, the world not an illusion to reject, but a radiant expression of the divine, with liberation simply the recognition that one is the single awareness pulsating in every heart.

Practice. The mantra Om Namaḥ Śivāya, "I bow to the Lord who is my own Self", is the heart of this stream's devotion: not surrender to an external deity, but to the infinite consciousness at the core of one's own being. Meditation on Śiva as the formless Liṅga, or as the silent presence in the heart, leads toward the direct recognition of the Ātman; the deepest practice of this stream is self-inquiry itself, "Who am I?"

1.5.2 Vaiṣṇava, Viṣṇu, Krishna, Rama, and the Sustaining Lord

The Vaiṣṇava stream approaches Brahman as Viṣṇu, the sustaining, loving Lord who upholds the world and who takes birth, age after age, wherever dharma has weakened, and through his principal forms: Krishna, the playful, all-attractive teacher of the Bhagavad Gītā; Rama, dharma lived out fully in an actual human life, steady under every trial; Nārāyaṇa and Vāsudeva, names for the same supreme Lord in his most all-encompassing aspect; and Rādhā, the devotion that dissolves itself entirely in love of the beloved.

This stream teaches that the world is sustained, not merely tolerated, by the divine, that existence itself is an act of ongoing grace, not indifference. It also teaches that ordinary love, for a spouse, a friend, a child, can itself become a doorway to the love of God, since love rightly directed does not need to be extinguished on the path to liberation, only deepened and widened.

Rama's sixteen qualities. When the sage Nārada is asked by Vālmīki whether anyone alive possesses every mark of a fully realized human being, he answers: yes, Rama. The sixteen qualities named are virtue, heroic strength, knowledge of dharma, gratitude, truthfulness in speech, firmness in vows, noble character, devotion to the welfare of all beings (including animals, Sugrīva the monkey king, Jaṭāyu the eagle, Jāmbavān the bear king all stand within Rama's circle of care), learning, capability, a bearing whose inner state shows outwardly, self-possession (his identity rooted in the Ātman rather than the ego, which is why exile, loss, and war could not fundamentally destabilize him), conquest of anger, luminosity (advanced practitioners are consistently described as visibly radiant, a face in which ego-anxiety has genuinely diminished registers differently to others), freedom from jealousy, and a righteousness whose anger, when finally roused by genuine injustice, is itself formidable.

Hanuman, who serves Rama not as subordinate but as the supreme expression of devotion, adds absolute loyalty, tireless service, and a devotion so complete that the marks of the mature devotee (described in Book Two) might have been written with him in mind. His declaration, "by body, by speech, by mind I am Rama's servant", is the tradition's most memorable statement of complete self-offering.

Krishna and the Gītā. Krishna's teaching to Arjuna on the battlefield is the philosophical and ethical backbone of this entire path (see Book Two, §2.6–2.7, for its central portraits). Krishna is worshipped both as the historical teacher of the Gītā and as Vāsudeva, the recognition, stated at the Gītā's own climax, that "Vāsudeva is everything" (Vāsudevaḥ sarvam iti): the ultimate non-dual recognition arrived at through the devotional route.

Practice. Om Namo Nārāyaṇāya and the simpler Rāma, Rāma, Rāma (the Tāraka mantra) are the classical entries into this stream. Devotion here typically emphasizes surrender (śaraṇāgati), remembrance of the divine name, and seeing love for family and community as continuous with, rather than opposed to, love of God.

1.5.3 Śākta, The Goddess and Her Bhairavas

The Śākta stream worships the Goddess, Devī, in her many forms: Durgā, Kālī, Gaurī, Rādhā, Sītā, Tripurā, and the wisdom-goddesses Riddhi, Siddhi, and Buddhi. She is the power without which the still witness could not act at all, as the tradition says, Śiva without Śakti cannot even move. She is never worshipped as subordinate to any god; she is Brahman itself in the mode of power, will, and action.

The three supreme Śaktis. Before her many forms emerge, the one Goddess divides her infinite power into three fundamental modes, not separate beings, but three faces of a single Śakti identical with Brahman:

  • Mahāsarasvatī, the power of jñāna, knowledge and the luminous word (vāk). Goddess of the Vedas, of sacred speech, and of the intellect that discerns real from unreal. White, luminous; holds a vīṇā, a book, a rosary. Her Bhairava is the formless, all-pervading śabda-brahman, reality expressed as sound.
  • Mahālakṣmī, the power of icchā, divine will and the sustaining abundance of the universe. The golden goddess of prosperity, love, and beauty, not mere worldly wealth but the soul's deep longing for union with the divine, the creative urge giving rise to all form. Her Bhairava is Viṣṇu as cosmic preserver.
  • Mahākālī, the power of kriyā, action, transformation, and the momentum of time. The dark, all-devouring force that dissolves creation back into its source, the dynamism without which nothing could change, grow, or be released; the current that carries the soul from ignorance to liberation. Her Bhairava is Mahākāla, the transcendent Śiva who stands beyond time itself.

The eternal pairs. From this triune Śakti spring countless forms, each with a Bhairava embodying the silent, witnessing awareness, power and consciousness eternally one:

  • Kālī and Mahākāla, Kālī, whose name comes from kāla (time), is the fierce dissolver of illusion and ego, standing on the inert form of Śiva to show that without her power even the still witness cannot move. Her garland of skulls represents the alphabet, the seeds of all language. Mahākāla is the Great Time beyond even the cosmic cycles.
  • Tripurā and Paramāśiva, Tripurā ("beauty of the three cities") is the goddess of the three states of consciousness, the witness-Self itself. The Tripurā Rahasya teaches that Tripurā is one's own innermost Self, and Paramāśiva the silent ground from which she arises; the Śrī Yantra's interlocking triangles are their union.
  • Durgā and Mahādeva, Durgā, the invincible warrior born from the combined power of all the gods, is the refuge of the distressed (her name means "fortress"); her Bhairava Mahādeva is the gentle, benevolent Śiva, the peace that follows the victory she wins over inner demons, pride, anger, greed.
  • Sītā and Rāma, Sītā, endurance and unwavering love under the harshest pressure, is the soul longing for the Lord; her reunion with Rāma after exile is liberation itself, the recognition that lover and beloved were never separate.
  • Rādhā and Krishna, Rādhā, the supreme devotee and embodiment of ecstatic love (prema-bhakti), is Krishna's bliss-giving power (hlādinī-śakti); from the non-dual standpoint she is individual consciousness dissolved in love, and their dance is the one Self delighting in its own reflection.
  • Gaurī and Śiva, Gaurī, the gentle, radiant form of Pārvatī, is domestic harmony and purity, who through penance draws the great ascetic Śiva back into engagement with creation; their union is power and consciousness meeting at the heart of the household.
  • Riddhi, Siddhi, Buddhi and Gaṇeśa, prosperity, spiritual perfection, and wisdom, held in balance by Gaṇeśa (detailed in §1.5.4 below), together grounding a complete life: material, spiritual, and intellectual at once.

On women. The teaching that all women are the embodiment of the Goddess is taken with full seriousness in this community: devaluation of women, or of any group, is a direct contradiction of this path, not a minor lapse. To harm a woman is, in the Śākta understanding, to harm the Goddess herself; to honor her is to honor the divine in its most immediate earthly expression.

Śāstra as Guru. In this stream, the ultimate Guru is not any human teacher but the divine word (vāk) itself, the Devī Gītā declares that the Goddess's words are the scripture, and the scripture is her breath. Even where a human teacher is present, their authority rests entirely on their capacity to transmit truth clearly, never on personality or lineage. This is precisely why Garjanaa, worshipping no single human guru, treats the texts themselves as teacher, with the community's shared, honest inquiry as the field where that teaching unfolds.

The world as līlā. The Devī Gītā teaches that the universe is the Goddess's spontaneous, joyful self-expression, not created from need, but from the sheer bliss of her own being. She is Brahman itself: "I am pure consciousness, the witness of the three states; the universe arises in me, is sustained by me, and dissolves into me." Her māyā has two functions, veiling her true nature, and projecting the appearance of the world's diversity, yet she herself remains as untouched by this as the sun is untouched by what it illuminates.

Practice. Mantras include Om Dum Durgāyai Namaḥ, Om Krīṃ Kālikāyai Namaḥ, and simple nāma-mantras to any chosen form (see the full table in Book Three, §3.11). The Goddess asks, in her own words, for devotion, knowledge, and selfless action together: seeing the divine in all beings, serving the suffering, and practicing non-violence, truthfulness, and compassion, with the highest knowledge being the direct recognition "I am the Goddess", which cuts the knot of ignorance at its root.

1.5.4 Gāṇapatya, Gaṇeśa as Brahman

The Gāṇapatya stream worships Gaṇeśa, the elephant-headed remover of obstacles, as the Supreme Reality itself, Brahman, from whom the universe arises and into which it dissolves. This vision is expressed most fully in the Gaṇeśa Gītā, embedded in the Gaṇeśa Purāṇa, modeled directly on the Bhagavad Gītā: a dialogue between Gaṇeśa and King Vareṇya on a battlefield, echoing Krishna and Arjuna at Kurukṣetra, in which Gaṇeśa reveals himself as the non-dual Self of all.

Gaṇeśa as Nirguṇa Brahman. He declares himself the one without a second, pure self-luminous consciousness, existing before creation, the source from which the universe arises, by which it is sustained, and into which it merges back, Sat-Chit-Ānanda, the witness of the three states, the turīya beyond them, the innermost Self of every being. In this he aligns exactly with the Upaniṣadic and Gītā teaching, using his own accessible, beloved form as the personal face of the impersonal absolute.

The symbolism of his form. Every feature of Gaṇeśa's iconic image is itself a teaching:

  • The elephant head, the elephant is the most discerning of animals; the head symbolizes the all-knowing intellect (buddhi) merged with the infinite, the cosmic wisdom perceiving oneness behind diversity.
  • The large ears, the primacy of listening (śravaṇam), the first step of the Vedāntic path; a true seeker listens deeply before speaking.
  • The trunk, capable of lifting a blade of grass or uprooting a tree, it embodies discrimination (viveka), the power to handle both the subtle and the gross and choose what is beneficial.
  • The single tusk, Gaṇeśa broke off one tusk to write down the Mahābhārata; it represents non-duality, the one truth remaining once all duality is sacrificed, and the willingness to sacrifice for the sake of knowledge.
  • The pot-belly, the cosmic womb, containing the entire universe within itself yet remaining unperturbed, Brahman as the vastness that holds all creation without strain.
  • The sweet (modaka) in his hand, the bliss of the Self, the ānanda that follows from knowing one's true nature.
  • The mouse at his feet, the restless, nibbling mind, full of small desires, now subdued and turned into a vehicle: the mind mastered rather than indulged, made a powerful servant rather than a compulsive master.

Core teachings of the Gaṇeśa Gītā. Across its eighteen chapters, mirroring the Gītā's structure: the Self is unborn and immortal, untouched by the body's changes, and grief arises only from mistaken identification with the perishable. Duty must be performed without attachment to results, all action offered to the Lord, so the heart is purified and made fit for knowledge. The highest path is direct recognition of identity with Brahman, "the wise see Me in all beings and all beings in Me." Devotion with form and attributes purifies the mind and develops single-pointed focus, culminating in the recognition that devotee and Lord were never two. The world, with all its diversity, is a projection of divine power (māyā), neither absolutely real nor utterly unreal, a play of consciousness moved through, as the Gītā says elsewhere, like a lotus leaf on water. And the seeker must rise beyond even sattva, the quality of goodness itself, abiding as the witness beyond all three guṇas.

Vighneśvara, remover of obstacles. Popularly invoked before any undertaking, Gaṇeśa's obstacle-removal is understood, in the Advaitic reading, at its deepest level: the greatest obstacle is avidyā itself, ignorance of one's true nature, and the ordinary obstacles of life, sickness, failure, conflict, are ultimately its symptoms. When Gaṇeśa removes an obstacle, he aligns the seeker's life so the mind can turn inward; the final obstacle he removes is the ego itself, the sense of being a separate self, and what remains when it falls is the boundless awareness that is Gaṇeśa's own nature.

Practice. Om Gaṃ Gaṇapataye Namaḥ is simple, direct, and complete, an invocation appropriate before any practice, difficult conversation, or undertaking, clearing the ground before the work begins. One need not be a scholar to begin with Gaṇeśa: a heartfelt offering, the chanting of his name, a moment of mindful silence before an image, are all sufficient doorways.

1.5.5 Saura, Sūrya, the Sun, as the Symbol of Brahman

The Saura stream worships Sūrya, the Sun, not merely as a star giving physical light and life, but as the supreme visible symbol of Brahman, the one self-effulgent awareness illuminating the entire universe.

Why the Sun. Three qualities of the physical Sun make it the tradition's most fitting natural symbol for the absolute: it is self-luminous, needing no other light to shine, exactly as Brahman is self-effulgent consciousness (svayam-prakāśa), not borrowing its existence or its light from anything else, it is the light by which mind, senses, and world are known, the unseen seer enabling all perception while remaining untouched by what it perceives. It illuminates all objects without discrimination, exactly as Brahman's presence makes no distinction between one being and another. And it is one, yet its light pervades everywhere at once, exactly as Brahman is one without a second, while the many individual souls are reflections of that single consciousness within the limiting adjuncts of the mind. The simple invocation Om Sūryāya Namaḥ, "salutation to Bhagavān Sūrya", invokes this very light as inner wisdom, not merely outer heat.

The Sun as the eye of the world and the Self. The Upaniṣads call the Sun jagat cakṣuḥ, the eye of the world; just as the physical eye cannot see without light, the mind cannot know without the light of consciousness, and that consciousness is the true Sūrya, the innermost Self, present equally as the Sun's brilliance in the sky and as awareness in every being. When the Saura devotee offers water and flowers to the rising Sun, the inner meaning is: this outward gesture is offered to the one awareness that is both the light in the sky and the light in the heart. The outer ritual trains the mind toward the inner recognition.

Further teaching pointers. The Sun gives life to all without expecting return, as Brahman sustains existence as pure grace. It is unchanging even as its light drives change in the world, as the Self is the eternal witness while body, mind, and world undergo constant flux. Its light is one, yet reflects in countless drops of water, rivers, and lakes, as the many individual souls are reflections of the single consciousness. It dispels darkness without ever being touched by it, as the Self destroys ignorance simply by being what it is, needing no battle. And it appears to rise and set while in fact never moving, the Earth turns, not the Sun, exactly as the Self appears to be born and die while it is only the body that changes; the Self is ever-present, ever-awake.

Practice. The nāma-mantra Om Sūryāya Namaḥ and the discipline of Sūrya Namaskāra (Book Three, §3.1) belong centrally to this stream. A simple meditation: gaze at the rising or setting Sun with half-closed eyes, absorbing its light while silently repeating Om Sūryāya Namaḥ; or, eyes closed, visualize a brilliant Sun in the space of the heart (hṛdaya-sūrya), the Ātman, the pure awareness illuminating all thought and feeling, felt as one's actual identity rather than the body or mind.

1.6 On the Equal Standing of the Five Streams

It is a real error, and a common one, to let devotion to one form curdle into a sense of rivalry with another. Kālī and Krishna, Śiva and the Goddess, Gaṇeśa and Sūrya are not different powers competing for a devotee's loyalty. They are one light, seen through five windows, the same Brahman approached through five doors built for five different temperaments.

A practitioner is never required to worship all five, or even more than one. What is asked is only this: that whichever form draws your heart, you hold it as a complete and sufficient path to the one reality, and extend the same respect to the doors that others have chosen to walk through.

BOOK TWO, YOGA KHAṆḌA

The Part on Union

2.1 Jñāna and Bhakti: Two Names for One Movement

A common and damaging misunderstanding treats knowledge (jñāna) and devotion (bhakti) as two competing paths, one cold and philosophical, the other warm and emotional, each implicitly critical of the other. This Smriti rejects that framing entirely. Jñāna and Bhakti are two names for a single movement of the same consciousness: the movement by which awareness stops mistaking itself for something small and separate, and recognizes itself as what it has always actually been.

Devotion, honestly followed to its own conclusion, does not stop at loving a form of the divine held at arm's length. It moves, through longing, surrender, and the gradual softening of the sense of a separate self who is doing the loving, toward exactly the recognition that knowledge names directly: that the worshipper, the worshipped, and the love between them are not, at the deepest level, three things but one. The Gītā states this without hedging: among all devotees, the Jñāni, the one who knows, is called the most beloved, precisely because their devotion has become ananya, undivided, no longer requiring the felt distance between lover and Beloved that ordinary devotion still assumes.

Knowledge, in turn, when it is not merely intellectual, when it is not simply the ability to repeat Aham Brahmāsmi correctly, requires exactly what devotion cultivates: humility before something greater than the calculating mind, sincerity that does not perform, and the trust (śraddhā) to continue practicing before the direct experience has arrived to confirm the practice was worthwhile. Dry intellectual Vedānta, pursued without any of the heart's engagement, reliably produces arrogance rather than freedom, the Gītā's own warning about Dambha, pride performed as spiritual attainment (see §2.8 below).

So: a practitioner is never asked to choose between "the path of knowledge" and "the path of devotion" as though they were two different destinations. They are asked to notice which end of the same rope their own temperament naturally grips first, and to trust that pulling on it will, if done honestly, bring the other end along.

2.2 Why the Formless Needs a Face

This has already been stated in Book One (§1.2) as a matter of doctrine; here it is stated as a matter of practice. The mind that tries to leap directly to formless inquiry, skipping devotion entirely, usually finds that its "meditation on the formless" quietly becomes meditation on its own boredom, restlessness, or subtle self-congratulation. The heart needs an object before it can be asked to eventually let go of every object.

This is why this Smriti recommends, for nearly every practitioner, beginning with a chosen form (Iṣṭa Devatā) from among the five streams described in Book One, and building a genuine relationship with that form, through mantra, through the touch-based consecration of the body (nyāsa), through offering, through the felt experience of turning toward something loved. The Advaita horizon toward which the whole path moves (§2.3 below) is never abandoned as a goal. It is simply not usually reached by refusing devotion at the outset; it is reached by devotion followed all the way through.

2.3 Ātma-bhāvita Bhakti: Devotion Grounded in the Self

Ātma-bhāvita bhakti, devotion whose ground and object is ultimately recognized as the Self, is the specific form of devotion this Smriti asks its practitioners to grow into. It differs from devotion that stops at the level of a transactional relationship with an external deity (asking for favors, fearing punishment, seeking to please a distant authority) in three ways:

First, it holds the Iṣṭa Devatā not as a being separate from and superior to the practitioner in the ordinary worldly sense, but as the practitioner's own deepest nature, approached in a form the heart can love. To chant Śivāya Namaḥ is, in this understanding, to greet the awareness that witnesses one's own waking, dreaming, and sleeping, not to petition an external ruler.

Second, it treats the felt experience of devotion, longing, tenderness, awe, surrender, as valuable data about the practitioner's own consciousness, not merely as emotion to be indulged or performed. The question ātma-bhāvita bhakti keeps returning to is not only "how do I feel toward my Iṣṭa?" but "who is the one feeling this?", which is Vichāra, self-inquiry (Book Three, §3.5), quietly folded into devotional practice itself.

Third, it does not resist its own dissolution. Ordinary devotion often wants the sweetness of separation to continue indefinitely, the lover wants to keep loving the Beloved from a slight distance, because the distance is part of what makes the loving feel like an activity being performed. Ātma-bhāvita bhakti is willing to let that distance close. Its natural end, as the Gītā, the Devī Gītā, and the Gaṇeśa Gītā all separately affirm, is the direct recognition that the devotee and the divine were never two, at which point devotion does not end so much as it stops requiring an object, becoming instead the simple, undivided love that consciousness has for its own being.

In practice, this means: whichever form you have chosen from Book One, hold it wholeheartedly, worship it through the concrete practices of Book Three, and periodically, in Ātma-anveṣaṇa, in the quiet after japa, turn the same loving attention that you gave the form back onto the one who has been loving it. You will not find two things there. This is not a failure of your devotion; it is devotion's actual completion.

2.4 The Four Yogas as One Yoga

The tradition names four primary paths of practice, corresponding to four faculties of the human person, and Garjanaa integrates all four rather than treating any as sufficient alone:

  • Jñāna Yoga, the path of knowledge: direct investigation of the self's nature through Vichāra, Śravaṇam (hearing), Mananam (reflection), and Nididhyāsanam (deep, sustained meditation on what has been heard and reflected upon).
  • Bhakti Yoga, the path of devotion: transformation through love and surrender toward a chosen form of the divine, dissolving the ego not through intellectual understanding but through the expansion of love until the lover-beloved distinction collapses (§2.3 above).
  • Karma Yoga, the path of action: acting fully, without attachment to results and without the ego's claim of authorship, every action offered as worship. This is the path uniquely available to those who cannot withdraw from ordinary activity, which is to say, nearly everyone.
  • Rāja Yoga, the path of mind-mastery: Patañjali's eight-limbed system, moving from ethical foundation through physical stability, breath, sense-withdrawal, and concentration into meditative absorption.

A practitioner drawn primarily to devotion will find that Bhakti prepares the ground for Jñāna. A practitioner drawn primarily to knowledge will find that without Bhakti's warmth and Karma Yoga's ethical grounding, intellectual understanding remains sterile, precisely the dry Vedānta warned against in §2.1. The practical sequence of daily practice given in Book Three integrates all four at once: Karma Yoga through service and ethical conduct, Bhakti through mantra and kīrtan, Rāja Yoga through the formal sitting sequence, and Jñāna Yoga through self-inquiry and textual study.

2.5 The Threefold Test, Applied to Teaching Itself

Book One (§1.3) introduced Śabda, Yukti, and Anubhūti as the standard by which any claim in this community is tested. It is worth restating here, in the context of union: no teacher, however senior, however moving their devotion or however precise their philosophy, is exempt from this test, and neither is any inner experience the practitioner has, including experiences that feel like breakthroughs. A powerful devotional experience that cannot, on reflection, be squared with the source texts and with plain reason is not automatically discarded, but it is held provisionally, not treated as final authority. This is what keeps Ātma-bhāvita Bhakti from sliding into private fantasy, and what keeps Jñāna Yoga from sliding into cold abstraction detached from lived practice.

2.6 The Values That Mark the Path

The values below are not a separate ethical system bolted onto the metaphysics of Book One or the practices of Book Three. They are what jñāna and bhakti, followed honestly, actually produce in a person along the way, the visible signs that the union described above is taking root. They are drawn from named texts and arranged in a deliberate order: from basic ethical restraint, through psychological maturity, to the fullest portraits of liberation.

The Three Da, The Thunder's Teaching

(Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad)

The creator-figure Prajāpati teaches three different kinds of beings with a single syllable: Da.

  • To the gods, prone to excess: Damyata, restrain yourself.
  • To humans, prone to hoarding: Datta, give.
  • To the demons, prone to cruelty: Dayadhvam, be compassionate.

All three apply to every practitioner, and all three have an inward form that matters just as much: Datta inward is releasing the fixed stories you tell about yourself, the ego's own hoarding of a self-narrative. Damyata inward is restraining the compulsive internal commentary that never stops judging. Dayadhvam inward is extending to yourself the same compassion you would extend to someone else.

The Three Gates to Ruin

(Bhagavad Gītā 16.21)

The Gītā names three forces that most reliably undo whatever has been built, and each is, in effect, the shadow side of one of the Three Da:

  • Kāma (compulsive desire), the opposite of restraint (Damyata): a fever of wanting that never lets satisfaction arrive.
  • Krodha (reactive anger), the opposite of compassion (Dayadhvam): the Gītā traces its exact chain, craving leads to attachment, attachment to frustration, frustration to anger, anger to delusion, delusion to loss of memory, and from there to the destruction of understanding itself.
  • Lobha (greed), the opposite of giving (Datta): the drive to acquire, which shows up in spiritual life (status, initiations, recognition) as readily as in material life.

The Gītā's instruction is direct: abandon these three.

Daivī Sampadā and Āsuri Sampadā, Two Directions

(Bhagavad Gītā 16)

The Gītā describes two directions a life can take, not two types of people, but two pulls present in everyone at once.

The Divine Qualities (Daivī Sampadā) surface as the ego's grip loosens: fearlessness, purity of heart, steadiness in practice, generosity, self-restraint, willingness to serve, love of study, tolerance for hardship, simplicity, non-harm, truthfulness, freedom from anger, non-attachment, peace, freedom from fault-finding, compassion, freedom from greed, gentleness, modesty, resolve, vigor, forgiveness, fortitude, cleanliness, and freedom from envy and excessive pride. These are not virtues to perform. They are what a mind naturally looks like once it is no longer run by anxiety.

The Demonic Qualities (Āsuri Sampadā) surface when the ego runs unexamined: pride disguised as virtue, arrogance, over-identification with the ego, anger, cruelty, and willful ignorance. (These are treated at length, with specific antidotes, in Book Four, §4.9, since this is where a community must actually guard against them.)

2.7 The Portraits of a Life Transformed

The tradition does not leave "transformation" vague. It offers precise, tested descriptions of what a person actually looks like at various depths of this union of knowledge and devotion. These are not aspirational fictions; they are diagnostic tools a practitioner can use honestly on themselves.

The Sthitaprajña, The Person of Steady Wisdom

(Bhagavad Gītā 2.54–72)

When the ego's compulsive patterns quiet down, a specific shift becomes visible. The Sthitaprajña has let go of the mind's manufactured desires and finds contentment in the Self; is not shattered by suffering, nor chases pleasure; has genuinely reduced attachment, fear, and anger through practice, not suppressed them; withdraws from sense-objects the way a tortoise draws in its limbs, naturally, not by force; and acts fully and completely, but without the constant inner refrain of "I did this, this is mine." This is not a distant ideal. It is what sincere practice actually produces.

The Bhakta Lakṣaṇas, Marks of the Mature Devotee

(Bhagavad Gītā 12.13–20)

Where the Sthitaprajña describes an inner state, the Bhakta Lakṣaṇas describe what that state looks like in relationship with others. The mature devotee holds no hatred toward any being, without exception; is naturally friendly and compassionate, not as effort but as the plain expression of a softened heart; holds people and outcomes without possessiveness or rigid self-interest; neither disturbs the world nor is disturbed by it; and is free from excitability, envy, fear, and agitation. These are not rare or exotic traits. They are the ordinary fruit of real practice.

The Guṇātīta, Beyond the Three Qualities

(Bhagavad Gītā 14.21–27)

Every mental state is built from three qualities: sattva (clarity), rajas (activity and restlessness), and tamas (dullness and inertia). Most people are simply carried along by whichever quality currently dominates. The Guṇātīta watches all three move, in themselves and in the world, without being driven by them: when clarity rises, they do not cling to it; when restlessness surges, they neither indulge nor suppress it; when dullness descends, they neither collapse into it nor fight it anxiously. Clay, stone, and gold register with the same inner evenness. This is achieved not by willpower but through ananya-bhakti, undivided devotion, which is precisely the mechanism described in §2.1 above.

The Jñāni, The One Who Knows

(Bhagavad Gītā 7.17–19)

Of all devotees, the Gītā names the Jñāni as most cherished, not for having more information, but because their very sense of identity has been transformed. They have recognized, not merely accepted intellectually, that the self and the divine were never two things. The Jñāni is living proof that the union this whole path aims at is actually possible.

Jīvanmukti, Liberation While Alive

(Yoga Vāsiṣṭha)

The Yoga Vāsiṣṭha's account of the Jīvanmukta, one liberated in this very body, this very life, is remarkable for its ordinariness. The Jīvanmukta works, eats, sleeps, laughs, grieves. The difference is entirely on the inside: no craving for what hasn't arrived, no aversion to what has; the old opposites (honor and shame, gain and loss, success and failure) no longer split them in two; "I" and "mine" remain as functional conveniences, not objects of desperate attachment; action happens without the exhausting sense of being its author; whatever arises passes through without leaving residue, like water off a lotus leaf; and they are quietly, genuinely cheerful, not performing peace, but free of the accumulated weight that made peace impossible before.

Vāsiṣṭha's central teaching: Jīvanmukti is not a state you win and then have to defend. It is a recognition that, once stable, needs no defending at all.

The Four Gatekeepers of Liberation

(Yoga Vāsiṣṭha)

Four conditions, present together, open the door to liberation:

  • Śama, tranquility of mind: not blankness or suppression, but a mind whose default setting is clarity rather than turbulence.
  • Santoṣa, genuine contentment: not resignation, but a settled relationship with what's actually here, free of the drive to acquire more. Santoṣa is the soil every other virtue grows in.
  • Vichāra, self-inquiry: direct investigation into the nature of the one having this experience, not thinking about yourself but turning attention toward the source of the sense of "I."
  • Satsaṅga, the company of the wise: not a social nicety. Even brief contact with genuine Satsaṅga can redirect an entire life; sustained exposure keeps a practitioner's orientation alive.

These four reinforce each other: Satsaṅga feeds Vichāra; Vichāra produces Śama; Śama makes Santoṣa possible; Santoṣa deepens the openness that makes Satsaṅga worthwhile in the first place.

The Sword of Aṣṭāvakra

(Aṣṭāvakra Gītā 1.2)

The most uncompromising text in the tradition does not build gradually toward liberation, it points at it immediately: "If you desire liberation, my child, then renounce sense-objects as poison and cultivate forgiveness, straightforwardness, compassion, contentment, and truthfulness as nectar."

These five, forgiveness, straightforwardness, compassion, contentment, truthfulness, are precise antidotes to the ego's grip: forgiveness dissolves the resentment that feeds the story of the wronged self; straightforwardness removes the cost of maintaining a performance; compassion dissolves the boundary the ego insists on between self and other; contentment dissolves the forward-reaching hunger that defines the ego's relationship to time; truthfulness dissolves the constructed narratives the ego runs on. Cultivated together, they leave the ego very little to work with.

Samānya Dharma, Conduct Over Experience

(Śrīmad Bhāgavatam)

The Bhāgavatam insists that sincere practice shows up in daily conduct, not private experience. A practitioner who meditates faithfully but is dishonest in business, unkind to family, or indifferent to those who cannot repay kindness has not yet integrated the practice. The Bhāgavatam's saints, Prahlāda, Dhruva, Kapila, are offered as proof of what real practice looks like in an ordinary life, not as extraordinary exceptions to it. (This principle governs the entirety of Book Four.)

The Ten Marks of Dharma

(Manu Smṛti)

A classical definition of universal conduct, offered as applying to everyone regardless of birth: steadiness, forgiveness, self-control, non-stealing, purity, restraint of the senses, sound understanding, knowledge, truthfulness, and freedom from anger.

Yama and Niyama, The Foundation of All Practice

(Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali 2.30–32)

Before Patañjali discusses meditation or absorption, he lays down the ethical foundation without which nothing else holds steady.

The Five Yamas (restraints): Ahiṃsā (non-violence, extending past physical harm into thought and speech); Satya (truthfulness, not just avoiding lies, but aligning your words with what you actually understand to be real); Asteya (non-stealing, extending past objects to not claiming credit, time, or attention that isn't yours); Brahmacharya (continence, directing vital energy toward practice rather than compulsive indulgence; for most people, choice rather than compulsion, not celibacy); Aparigraha (non-possessiveness, limiting acquisition to genuine need, and loosening the grip on what you already have).

The Five Niyamas (observances): Śaucha (purity of body, speech, and mind together); Santoṣa (contentment, the inner ground from which real action, free of craving, becomes possible); Tapas (austerity, accepting difficulty as part of growth); Svādhyāya (self-study, sustained engagement with liberating texts, aimed at transformation rather than information); Īśvara Praṇidhāna (surrender, offering the outcomes of your actions to the divine, rather than claiming them as your own).

2.8 The Two Directions: Daivī and Āsuri Sampadā, Revisited

Union, as described in this Book, is not automatic or guaranteed by ritual correctness. It is possible to perform every practice in Book Three flawlessly while the Āsuri Sampadā named in §2.6 quietly runs the show underneath, using devotion to feed pride, using knowledge to feed arrogance, using community standing to feed the ego's hunger for recognition. Book Four (§4.9) treats this at length, because it is precisely where a community, not just an individual practitioner, must build real safeguards. The two Books are companions: this one describes the direction practice is meant to move in; Book Four describes what to do when it doesn't.

The Part on Practice

"The body must be settled before the breath can be refined. The breath must be steadied before the mind can hold a mantra. The mind quieted by mantra is ready for self-inquiry. 3.0 Overview of the Ten Practices

The formal daily sitting has six parts, meant to be done in order, in a single unbroken arc, because each part prepares the ground for the next: the body is settled, then the breath, then the body is consecrated, then the mind is given a mantra to hold, then that quieted mind turns to inquire into its own source, and finally even the inquiry is released into open silence. A complete sitting takes 60–90 minutes; a minimum version, shortening each part rather than skipping any, can be done in 45.

Four more practices extend what the sitting produces into the whole of life: study of texts, participation in community, service, and the conduct of ordinary daily affairs, speech, relationships, work, money, and digital life.

None of the ten is optional in principle, though any may be shortened under real constraint. Attending only to the earlier, easier practices (posture, breath) while skipping the harder later ones (inquiry, silence) is arriving at the preparation and mistaking it for the practice itself.

3.1 Practice One, Sūrya Namaskāra and Āsana: Settling the Body

What to do. Perform the twelve-posture Sun Salutation as a moving prayer, silently or aloud pairing each posture with one of the twelve names of the sun (see §1.5.5 for the doctrinal grounding of this stream):

  1. Mitrāya Namaḥ, standing, hands at the heart.
  2. Ravaye Namaḥ, arms raised overhead, slight backbend.
  3. Sūryāya Namaḥ, forward fold, hands toward the earth.
  4. Bhānave Namaḥ, one foot back, opposite knee down, chest lifted.
  5. Khagāya Namaḥ, plank position.
  6. Puṣṇe Namaḥ, knees-chest-chin to the floor.
  7. Hiraṇyagarbhāya Namaḥ, cobra or upward-facing dog.
  8. Marīcaye Namaḥ, downward-facing dog.
  9. Ādityāya Namaḥ, the opposite foot steps forward into the lunge.
  10. Savitre Namaḥ, standing forward fold.
  11. Arkāya Namaḥ, arms swept overhead, slight backbend.
  12. Bhāskarāya Namaḥ, return to standing, hands at heart.

Repeat leading with the other foot to complete one full round; six rounds make a complete practice. Follow with whatever gentle hip-openers, forward folds, spinal twists, or backbends your own body needs that day, so that afterward you can sit still for fifteen to thirty minutes without physical distraction. That capacity to sit, not athletic performance, is the actual measure of whether this practice has done its job.

Why. Patañjali defines the purpose of āsana in two words: sthira-sukham, steady and comfortable (Yoga Sūtras 2.46). The body is not the goal; it is the vehicle. An unsettled body will continuously intrude on the subtler practices that follow, which is why the sequence begins here. Sūrya Namaskāra is held as devotion, not athletic performance: bowing to the sun at the start of practice is bowing to the inner light of awareness that the outer sun symbolizes (§1.5.5).

Duration: 15–30 minutes total.

3.2 Practice Two, Prāṇāyāma: Settling the Breath and Nervous System

Prāṇāyāma, prāṇa (life-force) + āyāma (extension, regulation), is not exercise in the fitness sense. The Yoga Vāsiṣṭha teaches: prāṇa and citta (mind) are the two wings of the same bird; control one and the other follows. Prāṇāyāma is the most accessible point of leverage on the mind available to a practitioner, more immediately effective than trying to control the mind directly, which is like trying to catch water in a net.

Kapālabhāti, Skull-Shining Breath (first, to clear morning dullness). Twenty to forty rapid, forceful exhales through the nose, letting the inhale happen passively. Build gradually over weeks. Caution: do not practice with high blood pressure, heart conditions, epilepsy, or during pregnancy without an experienced teacher's guidance.

Nāḍī Śodhana, Alternate Nostril Breathing (the core daily practice). Balances Iḍā (left, lunar, cooling) and Piṅgalā (right, solar, activating) nāḍīs, producing mental stillness and clarity that makes japa and meditation more accessible.

Sit with spine naturally erect. Rest the left hand on the left knee. Form Vishnu mudra with the right hand: fold the index and middle fingers toward the palm; thumb, ring finger, and little finger extended.

  1. Close the right nostril with the thumb. Inhale through the left for a count of four.
  2. Close both nostrils. Hold for four counts (later eight or sixteen, only as retention becomes genuinely comfortable, never strained).
  3. Release the thumb. Exhale through the right for eight counts.
  4. Inhale through the right for four.
  5. Close both. Hold for the same count as step 2.
  6. Release the ring finger. Exhale through the left for eight.

That is one round. Begin with five; build to ten, then twenty, over weeks and months. The ratio inhalation:retention:exhalation begins at 1:1:2 and, as practice deepens, may move toward 1:4:2. Retention should never be forced, a strained retention creates agitation, the opposite of the intent.

Bhrāmarī, Humming Bee Breath (for unusually agitated states). Close the ears gently with the thumbs, eyes lightly covered by the fingers, inhale through the nose, exhale with a sustained hum felt through the skull. Five rounds. The internal vibration directly interrupts compulsive thought.

Kumbhaka (retention) is developed only after months of comfortable Nāḍī Śodhana; exhale-retention only under experienced guidance. Never force a hold.

Duration: 10–20 minutes. Prāṇāyāma bridges the body-settled state produced by āsana and the mind-settling required for japa and meditation; it should not be rushed.

3.3 Practice Three, Nyāsa: Consecrating the Body

Nyāsa, from the root nyas, "to place or install", is an Āgamic practice in which the practitioner installs divine consciousness into the body through mantra and touch, performed before puja, before japa, and before deep meditation. The logic is Āgamic non-dualism in direct action: if Brahman pervades all existence, the body is already, in its ultimate nature, divine. Nyāsa does not import divinity into something profane; it ritually removes the obscuration of the body's sacredness, making explicit what was always implicit.

Forms, from simple to elaborate. Kara-nyāsa, installation on the fingers and palms, the instruments of practice. Aṅga-nyāsa, installation on the major limbs and regions (head, eyes, ears, mouth, heart, navel, knees, feet); in elaborate Āgamic practice this can involve dozens of placements with distinct mantras, but the simplified daily form uses heart, shoulders, eyes, and crown. Hṛdaya-nyāsa, installation centered on the heart alone, the most essential form: the heart, in Advaita and Āgamic understanding alike, is the seat of the Ātman, the place from which the sense of "I" arises and to which it returns in deep sleep.

A full-body daily practice. Beyond the simple heart-shoulders-eyes-crown sequence given for brief daily use, a fuller aṅga-nyāsa extends the same touch-and-mantra installation across the whole body, useful especially before extended sitting, before a rite of passage (Book Four, §4.6), or whenever the practitioner has more than the usual few minutes:

Touch, in sequence, while silently repeating the chosen mantra at each point: crown of the head → forehead (third eye point) → both eyes → both ears → nose → mouth → throat → both shoulders → heart → navel → both palms → both knees → both feet, closing by resting both hands over the heart with the recognition: this body, completely, is the dwelling of the divine.

A simple daily anga-nyāsa (for ordinary daily use, before japa): heart → right shoulder → left shoulder → right eye → left eye → crown of the head → close with both hands over the heart.

The intention in either form is not mechanical correctness but genuine recognition: this body is the dwelling of the divine; I enter practice from this understanding. Nyāsa marks the threshold between ordinary sitting and formal practice, and changes the quality of everything that follows.

Slogans (subhāṣita vākyas) used alongside practice, as reminders held in the mind rather than formal mantras: Yato Dharmaḥ Tato Jayaḥ, where there is dharma, there is victory; Satyameva Jayate, truth alone triumphs; Vāsudevaḥ sarvam, Vāsudeva is everything (see Book One, §1.5.2).

Duration: 3–5 minutes for the simple form; longer for the full-body form. Brief, but never to be skipped.

3.4 Practice Four, Japa and Kīrtan

The Community's Shared Mantra

Kālī-Durge Rādheśyām, Gaurīśaṅkar Sītārām

This mantra draws one name from each of three streams, in complementary pairs: Kālī and Durgā (Śākta, the transformative dissolver of illusion, and the victorious protector), Rādhe and Śyām (Vaiṣṇava, pure devotion embodied, and Krishna, the dark, playful divine), Gaurī and Śaṅkar (Śaiva, consciousness made soft, and Śiva as the auspicious, benevolent one), Sītā and Rām (Vaiṣṇava, dharma lived in a life, and the ideal, the delight of all hearts). Every pairing is a complementary unity: in Advaita terms, each pair points at one non-dual reality, named from two angles, holding apparent duality and its dissolution simultaneously. All names receive equal weight in the same line, no form is placed above another. It is also simply and reliably singable: the rhythm is natural and easy to learn, and a practitioner who can hum participates fully.

Japa, Solitary Repetition

Japa is sustained, meditative repetition done alone, silently or in soft undertone, using a mālā (108-bead prayer necklace).

The mālā: 108 beads strung with one larger Meru bead at the junction. One full round (108 repetitions) is the daily minimum.

Technique: Sit in the meditative posture established after prāṇāyāma and nyāsa. Rest the mālā in the right hand, draped across the ring finger, held by the thumb. The index finger never touches the mālā. Move bead to bead as each repetition completes. At the Meru bead, do not cross it, reverse direction if continuing.

The quality of japa. The mālā counts so the practitioner does not have to. Attention is entirely free for the mantra itself, for offering each repetition, for dwelling in the names, for allowing the mantra to work below conscious intention. Over time, the mantra begins to arise naturally in the background of ordinary activity, continuing quietly in the spaces between thoughts. When this happens, the mantra has taken root, the tradition calls this siddha, accomplished, self-sustaining. Understanding Sanskrit is not required; the names carry their own weight, and sincerity matters more than linguistic mastery.

Duration: 15–20 minutes minimum, approximately one full mālā round.

Kīrtan, The Community's Voice

Kīrtan is japa in its communal, musical, devotional form: the same mantra sung together, typically one person leading and the group responding, at a pace and rhythm that builds gradually. Where japa works through sustained, quiet individual attention, kīrtan works through the collective field of voice, rhythm, and shared intention, dissolving individual self-consciousness in a way solitary practice cannot replicate. This is Satsaṅga in one of its most immediate forms.

No musical training is required, the ability to hold a note and feel a pulse is entirely sufficient. The lead singer provides melody; the group sustains and responds. Simple percussion (hand drum, clapping, small cymbals) is traditional and sufficient.

The arc of a session: begin slowly and quietly; let the group settle and find each other's rhythm; gradually increase pace and energy, following the mantra's natural momentum; build to a peak, louder and more fully inhabited; return, equally gradually, to stillness; close in shared silence. The silence at the end is as important as the singing, it is the vessel into which the kīrtan's energy settles.

3.5 Practice Five, Ātma-anveṣaṇa: Self-Inquiry

Ātma-anveṣaṇa, investigation of the Self, is the culmination of the formal sitting. Everything preceding it (body, breath, consecration, mantra) is preparation for this. It is not thinking about the self, which only produces more content, more story; it is attention turned toward the consciousness that is the background and substance of all content, the awareness always already present, in which thinking itself appears. This is the practice form of Vichāra, one of the Four Gatekeepers named in Book Two (§2.7), and it is what ātma-bhāvita bhakti (Book Two, §2.3) quietly asks of devotion at every turn.

The mechanics, moment to moment:

  1. A thought, emotion, or sensation arises.
  2. Ask: to whom does this arise?
  3. The honest answer: to me.
  4. Ask: who am I?
  5. Do not reach for an intellectual answer. Let the question redirect attention back toward its source.
  6. Rest in that attending. When another thought arises, return with the question again. The returning, repeated, is the practice.

The classical forms of the question are Ko'ham? ("Who am I?") and Kutaḥ ayam aham-bhāvaḥ? ("Whence does this sense of 'I' arise?", the Ramaṇa Maharṣi formulation). The Mumukṣu-mantra, Aham Baddhaḥ Vimuktaḥ Syām ("I am bound; may I be free"), may also be held not as a statement of complaint but turned into a question, who is this "I" that claims to be bound?

The thread to follow: the aham-sphuraṇa, the felt, pre-conceptual sense of simply existing, not "I am a practitioner," not "I am sitting here," but the bare hum of being beneath every label the mind adds. This is not a relaxation technique, not a visualization, and not mere repetition of a phrase as a mantra, the attention required is alert and precise.

Duration: 10–20 minutes, following japa.

3.6 Practice Six, Chup-Sādhana: The Discipline of Silence

Where self-inquiry is active, a question held, a thread followed, this practice is deliberate non-doing: no technique applied, nothing sought, simply remaining. When inquiry has done its work in a given sitting, the practitioner stops applying the question and rests. Thoughts that arise are neither followed nor fought, they appear the way clouds appear in the sky, and the sky does not reach for them.

Managing dullness. If drowsiness creeps in, open the eyes slightly, straighten the spine, take one conscious breath, and return to alert stillness. Drowsiness is not this practice; genuine Chup-Sādhana is the alert stillness of a clear mind, not the fog of near-sleep.

Extended forms to build into a weekly and occasional rhythm: once weekly, extend the full sitting to 30–60 minutes; practice daily mauna (restraint of speech when speech is not truly needed); on occasion keep a half-day or full day of complete voluntary silence; at community gatherings, close every session with five minutes of shared silence, a collective stillness with a different quality than solitary silence, never to be cut short for time.

Duration: 5–15 minutes, following self-inquiry, to complete the formal sitting.

The complete formal sequence, Sūrya Namaskāra through Chup-Sādhana, takes 60 to 90 minutes at full length, or as little as 45 minutes with minimum durations. Do not shorten by skipping the final practices when time is constrained; shorten each part slightly instead. The final two practices address the root; the earlier ones are preparations.

3.7 Practice Seven, Svādhyāya: Studying the Texts

Reading here means reading in order to be changed, not merely to know more. After any passage, ask: what does this ask of my actual life this week? If you can answer that in one sentence, the text has been received. If you can only summarize its content, it has been encountered but not yet practiced.

A basic reading rotation. The Bhagavad Gītā, the Rāmāyaṇa of Vālmīki, the Mahābhārata, the Yoga Vāsiṣṭha, the Tripura Rahasya, the Aṣṭāvakra Gītā, the Avadhūta Gītā, the Śrīmad Bhāgavatam, the Śrīmad Devī Bhāgavatam and Devī Gītā, the Gaṇeśa Gītā, and the Upaniṣads (particularly Māṇḍūkya, Bṛhadāraṇyaka, Chāndogya, and Kena).

Priority within the Gītā, given its central place: Chapter 2 (the Sthitaprajña description and the foundations of non-attached action); Chapter 12 (the Bhakta Lakṣaṇas); Chapter 14 (the Guṇātīta); Chapter 16 (Daivī and Āsuri Sampadā as a practical diagnostic); Chapter 3 (Karma Yoga); and Chapters 7, 9, and 18 for the fuller synthesis.

A practitioner who has genuinely sat with all eighteen chapters once over a year, pausing after each verse to ask what it asks of this week, has engaged the Gītā more meaningfully than one who has read it ten times at speed.

3.8 Practice Eight, Community Gathering: Satsaṅga in Structure

Community is itself a practice, not merely a support for practice, because the ego can hide indefinitely in solitude but cannot hide for long in honest relationship. Satsaṅga is named as one of the Four Gatekeepers in Book Two (§2.7): not a benefit of the path but one of its non-negotiable conditions.

A tested two-hour format:

  • Opening (5–10 min): shared silence or one slow round of the community mantra, marking the transition into gathering-time.
  • Reading (10–15 min): one member reads a short, previously chosen passage from a core text.
  • Discussion (25–35 min): guided by one question, what does this ask of our actual lives this week?, not abstract philosophical debate. Everyone speaks from their own position; no one speaks to display understanding.
  • Kīrtan (25–35 min): built slowly to a peak and back to stillness, as in §3.4.
  • Closing silence (5–10 min): never sacrificed for time; it is the container into which the whole gathering settles.

Scale. A working gathering group should be 6–15 people, small enough that everyone is genuinely known.

Roles rotate and are never permanent or hereditary. A Gathering Facilitator holds the session's structure; a Study Coordinator chooses readings; a Kīrtan Lead holds the melody as a servant of the group's devotion, not a performer. No one holds any of these roles by virtue of knowledge, seniority, or charisma, and no arrangement that places one person consistently above question is acceptable. (The organizational implications of this are developed fully in Book Four.)

3.9 Practice Nine, Sevā: Service as Karma Yoga

Service is where non-attachment is genuinely tested, far more honestly than in meditation, because the person served may be difficult, unappreciative, or actively resistant, removing every condition the ego prefers.

Forms of service: the unglamorous logistics of running gatherings; sitting with a struggling community member without needing to fix, explain, or perform equanimity at them; teaching or mentoring newer practitioners in the spirit the Gītā describes, with reverence, inquiry, and service, never superiority; and service in the wider world (environmental care, practical help to those in need) done because the need exists, not to build the community's or the practitioner's reputation.

The honest test: does the act of service leave a residue, pride if it went well, resentment if unacknowledged, comparison with others' efforts? Noticing that residue honestly, without shame, and returning to the intention of simple offering, is the practice. The residue lessens over years of honest practice; that is the trajectory to expect, not instant purity.

The Three Da (Book Two, §2.6) are not contemplative in the abstract, they have hands and feet. Datta needs to actually give something; generosity that never manifests in actual giving is the intention of Datta, a beginning but not yet the practice. Dayadhvam needs to move toward the being that is suffering; compassion that feels but does not respond has not yet become what the tradition describes.

3.10 Practice Ten, Ordinary Life

This is the point of everything above. A separate "spiritual life" that coexists with an unchanged ordinary life has missed the purpose entirely. The Gītā's portraits, Sthitaprajña, Guṇātīta, Bhakta, describe how a mature practitioner moves through ordinary life, not retreat conditions, not a hermitage: the actual world, with its actual difficulty, friction, and people.

Speech. Before speaking, a genuine pause: is this true? Is it necessary? Is it kind? When these conflict, prioritize non-harm. Say what you mean; do not promise what you will not deliver; do not present a face to the world that does not match your interior state. Practice voluntary silence rather than filling every gap out of social anxiety.

Relationships. Notice reactions most acutely with the specific people who reliably provoke you, a difficult family member, a structurally opposed colleague, since this is where the practice is actually tested. Practice letting people be who they are rather than who you need them to be for your own comfort; this is often the last and hardest form of ego to release, because it wears the face of love. Excessive praise destabilizes the mind exactly as much as blame; treat both as information, not as verdicts.

Work and money. Do not take credit not earned or appropriate recognition belonging to others. Work with full engagement and genuine competence without the compulsive drive to accumulate beneath it, this does not require poverty, only a different interior relationship to what is earned and spent. Ask "to whom does this frustration arise?" at your desk exactly as you would on the meditation cushion.

Digital life. Before picking up a device: is this arising from clarity or from compulsion? Words typed in anger carry the same weight as words spoken; the relative anonymity of digital spaces is not a suspension of the practice of non-harm in speech. Build in periods of deliberate disconnection.

Encountering disagreement. One who is genuinely established in the practice feels little need to defend that establishment, the impulse to defend hard is itself useful information, not a cause for shame. This applies as much to the community as a whole: a community organized around proving itself to skeptics has made the skeptics, not the practice, its center of gravity.

3.12 How the Ten Practices Fit Together

Each of the ten practices addresses a different dimension of the practitioner's being. The formal sequence moves in a single arc from the gross body to open silence; the remaining practices expand what that arc produces into the rest of life.

The path is long. It demands genuine engagement, honest self-assessment, and the willingness to continue even when, especially when, the results are not immediate or visible. The tradition's unanimous teaching: the practice itself transforms the practitioner, whether or not the practitioner can see the transformation happening. Stay the course.

BOOK FOUR, ĀCĀRA KHAṆḌA

The Part on Conduct

4.1 The Spirit of Community Life

This code is not a rulebook imposed from above. It is a shared agreement, reflecting the knowledge of Book One, the union described in Book Two, and the practices of Book Three, put into the form of actual daily life together. It exists to help members walk the path with honesty, kindness, and clarity, to protect the vulnerable, hold the community accountable to its own stated ideals, and keep it a genuine Satsaṅga rather than drifting, as intense communities often do, into a sect or a personality cult. When members fall short, this code is meant to guide them back without shame, in the spirit of ongoing self-study (Svādhyāya, §3.7).

Non-negotiable core principles, applying to every gathering, communication, and relationship in this community:

  • Voluntary participation. No one is recruited, pressured, or manipulated into joining or staying.
  • No birth-based hierarchy. Spiritual maturity is shown by practice and conduct, never assumed from caste, gender, or birth (Book One, §1.4).
  • Accountability to texts. Any teaching must survive the Threefold Test, Śabda, Yukti, Anubhūti (Book One, §1.3), not charismatic authority.
  • Honesty and transparency. Financial arrangements, power dynamics, and personal agendas are never hidden behind spiritual language.
  • Non-harm. Physical, emotional, and psychological harm are guarded against; abuse of any kind is not tolerated.
  • No financial exploitation. Contributions are voluntary and transparent; no one profits personally from teaching or ritual beyond reasonable expense reimbursement.
  • Respect for the living tradition. Hereditary Hindu communities and other genuine paths are never disparaged; this community is a home for those who have none, not a competitor to those who already have one.

The Community as Satsaṅga. The heart of this community is Satsaṅga, not a social club, support group, or platform for personal ambition. We gather to practice, study, and hold each other accountable; social warmth is welcome but secondary. We speak to each other with honesty and care, gossip, backbiting, and passive aggression are recognized as expressions of the unexamined ego and are discouraged. We do not elevate any individual to the status of guru or unquestionable authority: teachers and elders are respected for their practice and wisdom, not worshipped, and all are fallible and subject to the same standards. We give and receive feedback as an act of service, an honest observation about your conduct, even if it stings, is a gift, not an attack.

4.2 Levels of Engagement

There is no single required path and no pressure to advance. Four stages describe, loosely, how involvement tends to deepen.

Inquirer (Jijñāsu). Exploring, attending open gatherings, asking questions, coming and going freely, with no commitment beyond basic respect for the space and the people in it. No disruptive behavior; no attempt to teach or represent the community publicly.

New Practitioner (Sādhaka). Has read the invitation and feels genuine resonance; begins a daily practice, however simple; attends gatherings with some regularity; is learning the texts and the community mantra. To become one, simply declare this intention to a facilitator or elder, or within a gathering, no ceremony required, though a simple blessing may be requested.

Expected: a basic daily sitting (even 20 minutes combining some āsana, prāṇāyāma, japa, and silence); regular attendance when possible; honest effort at the Three Da (Book Two, §2.6) and the Yamas and Niyamas; non-harm in speech and action within the community.

Serious Practitioner (Dṛḍha Sādhaka). Has walked the path a year or more, with practice as a central pillar of life; requests a conversation with at least two elders or established Serious Practitioners, a mutual discernment, not a test, and may then choose the Sādhana Dīkṣā ceremony (§4.6.6) as a public affirmation of a path already walked.

Expected: 45–60 minutes of daily formal practice following the full sequence of Book Three; consistent study of core texts; community participation and service; visible effort to embody the Daivī Sampadā and weaken the Āsuri Sampadā (§4.8); honesty about struggles rather than a performance of perfection; direct, non-gossiping conflict resolution.

Elder / Facilitator (Mārgadarśaka). A function, not a rank, arising from long practice, deep study, and natural aptitude, facilitating gatherings, coordinating study, leading kīrtan, mentoring newer practitioners, performing rites. Elders emerge through years of the community's own recognition, are subject to the same code as everyone else, must actively welcome feedback, and are immediately and publicly removed from the role if they use it for personal gain, sexual exploitation, or psychological control. The community must never tolerate the emergence of a cult figure.

4.3 Daily Conduct and Relationships

Speech. Speak what is true, useful, and kind; when these conflict, prioritize non-harm. Concerns about a person go to them, never about them behind their back. Community online forums are an extension of Satsaṅga; the same standards apply, avoid heated debate, performative displays of knowledge, and anonymous criticism.

Money. No fees, tithes, or dues are demanded; donations for shared expenses (rent, texts, supplies) are collected transparently, with financial records open to any member on request. No teacher or elder charges for teaching, initiation, or rituals, teaching is service. No one uses community connections to solicit funds for personal projects without full transparency and consent.

Romance and family. Romantic relationships between members are natural but must be conducted with full honesty and no power imbalance. An elder or facilitator must not be in a romantic or sexual relationship with someone they are actively mentoring; if such a relationship develops, the mentoring ends, and the situation is disclosed to another elder.

Inclusion. Open to all, regardless of gender, sexual orientation, race, ethnicity, or disability, given a sincere interest in the path. Sexism, misogyny, or devaluation of any group is contrary to Dharma and will not be tolerated, this follows directly from the Śākta teaching (Book One, §1.5.3) that all women embody the Goddess, and from the basic teaching (Book One, §1.4) that no one soul stands above another.

4.4 Nīti: The Wisdom of Worldly Conduct

Dharma, as this Smriti has described it so far, concerns the inner orientation of a life, the loosening of the ego, the recognition of the one Self, the cultivation of the divine qualities. But the tradition has never treated the inner life as though it floats free of the ordinary business of living among other people: earning, trusting, being deceived, choosing companions, keeping secrets, timing one's actions, and knowing when to act and when to wait. This is the domain of Nīti, practical, worldly wisdom, and the tradition offers three major voices on it that this community draws from directly: Cāṇakya, whose aphorisms address the individual's conduct in an uncertain world; Vidura, whose counsel to King Dhṛtarāṣṭra in the Mahābhārata addresses the specific burdens of one who must act wisely under pressure and in relationship; and Śukra, whose Nītiśāstra addresses conduct at the scale of households, alliances, and shared enterprises.

Nīti is not a separate ethics from the Dharma taught in Books One through Three. It is that same Dharma applied at street level, to the specific, practical question of how a sincere practitioner navigates trust, money, friendship, and conflict without either being exploited by the unscrupulous or becoming unscrupulous themselves in self-defense. A practitioner who has mastered Ātma-anveṣaṇa (§3.5) but who is naive about ordinary human deception has not yet completed the path; wisdom must operate at both levels simultaneously.

On Trust and Discernment

Chāṇakya's warning stands first, because it is the one most often ignored by sincere people: trust is not a virtue to be extended uniformly. Cāṇakya teaches that testing before trusting is not cynicism but prudence, a person should be observed in action, under pressure, and over time, before being given responsibility, confidence, or access to what matters. This is not paranoia; it is simply the recognition that good intentions in oneself do not guarantee good conduct in others, and that a community built entirely on the assumption of universal good faith becomes, in time, an easy target for the very Āsuri tendencies described in §4.9.

The specific test Cāṇakya offers is this: a person's true nature reveals itself not in comfortable circumstances but in four situations, in the handling of wealth, in a crisis, in hunger (that is, genuine scarcity or need), and at the time of one's own downfall. A person who remains honest, calm, and generous in these four conditions has demonstrated character; a person who has only ever been observed when comfortable has not yet been tested at all. This applies as much to a prospective elder or facilitator (§4.2) as to a prospective spouse (§4.5) or business partner.

Vidura's counsel to Dhṛtarāṣṭra adds a complementary caution: do not reveal your full mind carelessly to others. This is not a teaching of deception, it sits alongside, not against, the Yama of Satya (Book Two, §2.6), but a recognition that most inner thoughts, plans, and vulnerabilities are not yet ripe for sharing, and that indiscriminate self-disclosure is often less a mark of honesty than of poor judgment about timing and audience. A practitioner may be entirely truthful while still being discerning about when and to whom the fuller truth is spoken.

The Six Enemies and the Weaknesses of Judgment

Book Four (§4.9) has already named the six enemies from the Vidura Nīti, kāma, krodha, lobha, moha, mada, matsarya. Cāṇakya adds a specific, practical warning about how these six distort judgment in worldly dealings specifically: a person governed by desire misjudges what they can actually afford; a person governed by anger misjudges who is actually the threat; a person governed by greed misjudges the durability of a gain achieved by exploiting someone else's trust. The tradition's advice is not to suppress these six but, exactly as in the inner-defence practices of §4.9, to recognize their presence before acting from them, asking, in any significant transaction or decision, "which of the six enemies is currently speaking?"

On Alliance, Enmity, and Neutrality

From Śukra's Nītiśāstra, which addresses conduct at the scale of households, communities, and shared enterprises rather than the solitary individual, comes a teaching on how to correctly read one's actual position relative to others, not out of suspicion, but out of the plain necessity of acting wisely rather than sentimentally. Śukra names several possible relationships a person or group may have with another: alliance (shared purpose, mutual benefit), neutrality (no active relationship either way), and enmity (active opposition), and warns against the common error of mistaking one for another. Treating a genuine ally as a threat wastes goodwill and isolates the community unnecessarily; treating a genuine threat as a neutral party, out of a wish to see the best in everyone, leaves the community and its vulnerable members exposed. The Roaring Defence tradition within this Smriti (§4.10) already applies this discernment to hostile external systems; Śukra's teaching simply generalizes it, the same clear-eyed assessment is owed to individuals, not only to movements.

On timing and restraint in confrontation, Śukra and Cāṇakya agree: strength is not demonstrated by immediate confrontation of every wrong, but by the patience to act at the right moment, with adequate preparation, and, wherever possible, without unnecessary harm to the person being confronted. This is not passivity. It is the same discipline described in the Conflict Resolution process (§4.6): direct communication first, escalation only as genuinely required, and separation only as a last resort. Vidura's teaching that "the person who cannot wait, who demands immediate results from their circumstances and relationships, will fail at most of them" applies directly here, urgency is very often the ego's demand for relief, not a genuine requirement of the situation.

On Speech, Silence, and the Company One Keeps

Vidura's teachings on speech are among the tradition's most practical: words spoken in anger cannot be recalled, so truth should be spoken even when unwelcome, but with the timing and restraint that keep it from being merely destructive. The person who says what needs to be said, and refrains from saying what does not need to be said, has achieved something genuinely rare. This is the same discipline named in Book Three (§3.10) under "Speech," and Vidura's formulation adds a further test: before speaking a difficult truth, ask not only is it true, is it necessary, is it kind but also is this the right time, and am I the right person to say it? A true statement, badly timed or delivered by someone without standing to say it, often does more harm than a delayed one.

On the company one keeps, Cāṇakya is direct: a person takes on the qualities of those they spend the most time with, largely without noticing it happening. This is precisely why Satsaṅga (Book Two, §2.7; Book Three, §3.8) is named as one of the Four Gatekeepers rather than a mere social nicety, the tradition's own worldly wisdom confirms what the philosophical texts teach. A practitioner should be as deliberate about ordinary friendships and daily company as about formal community membership, since both shape the mind's habitual orientation more than any single decision does.

On Wealth, Work, and Practical Provision

Cāṇakya's teaching on wealth is blunt and unsentimental: a person should protect their wealth for times of difficulty, but should be willing to sacrifice wealth entirely to protect a spouse or dependents, and willing to sacrifice both wealth and dependents' comfort, if it truly comes to it, to protect their own integrity and their commitment to the path. This is not a teaching that ranks material security above dharma; it is a teaching about sequence, ordinary prudence in ordinary times, and clarity about what actually matters when ordinary times end. It sits comfortably alongside the teaching on Artha in Book Two (§2.6): prosperity pursued in conformity with Dharma is legitimate and, in fact, a genuine responsibility toward one's dependents, not a spiritual embarrassment to be denied.

Śukra's teaching on practical governance, originally addressed to those responsible for households, guilds, or small kingdoms, translates directly to the responsibilities of the Council of Elders (§4.8): those entrusted with shared resources or shared decision-making owe the group foresight (anticipating difficulty before it arrives, rather than only reacting to it), fair and transparent accounting (which this Smriti has already made structural, not merely aspirational, in §4.8), and the humility to seek counsel from those with relevant experience rather than relying solely on one's own judgment, however sincere.

On Enduring Hardship and Facing Downfall

Vidura's teaching to Dhṛtarāṣṭra on adversity offers the tradition's clearest worldly complement to the Sthitaprajña portrait (Book Two, §2.7): the wise person does not rejoice excessively at success or collapse under failure; is not broken by adversity and does not become arrogant in prosperity; acts promptly when genuine opportunity presents itself, without the hesitation born of fear of failure or fear of appearing to need something; and does not postpone what genuinely needs to be done, recognizing that delay is very often the ego's preferred strategy for avoiding the discomfort of honest action.

On gratitude and loyalty, both Cāṇakya and Vidura are emphatic in a way this Smriti fully affirms: those who have helped a person, especially in genuine difficulty, deserve that person's memory and continued good faith. Ingratitude, failing to acknowledge real kindness once it is no longer immediately useful, is named by Vidura as among the qualities most corrosive to character, precisely because it reveals that the earlier warmth was transactional rather than sincere. A practitioner tracks, honestly, whether their own regard for others rises and falls with what those others can currently offer, this is a direct and uncomfortable application of the Threefold Test (§1.3) to one's own social conduct, and a fair question for any Serious Practitioner to ask of themselves in the yearly self-audit already required of elders (§4.8).

How This Fits the Rest of the Path

None of the teachings above license coldness, manipulation, or the treatment of other people as instruments. Where any worldly teaching from Cāṇakya, Vidura, or Śukra appears to conflict with Ahiṃsā, Satya, or the basic dignity owed to every being under the recognition that all consciousness is one (Book One, §1.1), the metaphysical and ethical teaching governs, and the worldly teaching is set aside or reinterpreted. Nīti in this Smriti is a discipline of clear seeing in ordinary affairs, never a permission to abandon the deeper commitments of the path in the name of practicality. Its purpose is the same as everything else in this Book: to keep a sincere community, and the sincere individuals within it, from being naive in a way that ultimately harms both themselves and the people depending on their good judgment.

4.5 On Marriage: Definition and Practice

Definition. Marriage (Vivāha) in this community is defined as a voluntary, mutually chosen union between two practitioners, or between a practitioner and a partner who respects the practitioner's path even without sharing it, undertaken as a joint commitment to the four aims of life (Puruṣārtha: Dharma, Artha, Kāma, Mokṣa) and, specifically, as a partnership for practice, companionship, and, if children come, their dharmic upbringing.

Marriage here is not merely a legal or social contract, and not merely a vehicle for lawful pleasure or family continuity, though it fully includes and honors both. It is understood as a legitimate and complete expression of Karma Yoga: two people offering their combined life, including its ordinary and difficult parts, as a single act of practice. Neither partner is regarded as the possession of the other. The union does not exist to complete the ego of either partner, but to give both a companion in loosening it.

Who may marry. This community places no barrier of caste, birth, or background on marriage, consistent with §1.4 and §4.1. Both partners must enter freely and by their own informed choice; no marriage arranged through pressure, deception, or coercion is recognized as valid within this community's understanding, regardless of its legal status elsewhere. The community expects fidelity, honesty, and mutual respect within the marriage as ordinary ethical conduct (§4.3), not as a special or additional rule.

On genetically close unions. This community does not recognize as valid, and will not perform the Vivāha Saṃskāra for, a marriage between partners who are closely related by blood, parent and child, siblings, first cousins, or any relation of comparable genetic closeness, regardless of the customs of any lineage, region, or legal jurisdiction that may permit such unions. This prohibition rests on the same ground as every other teaching in this Smriti: Ahiṃsā, non-harm, extended specifically to children not yet born.

The reasoning is not superstition dressed as scripture, nor an appeal to disgust or custom; it is a direct application of the Threefold Test (§1.3). Anubhūti, the accumulated, testable evidence of human genetics, establishes plainly that children of closely related parents carry a substantially elevated risk of inherited disease and developmental harm, because harmful recessive traits, ordinarily masked when parents are unrelated, are far more likely to be shared and expressed when parents share recent ancestry. Yukti, reason applied to this evidence, leaves no honest room to treat such a union as harmless: to knowingly increase the likelihood of suffering in a child who has no voice in the decision is a plain violation of Ahiṃsā, whatever affection or private choice exists between the adults involved. Śabda, the tradition's own teaching, is not violated by this conclusion; nowhere do the source texts of this path command marriage between close kin, and where local custom has permitted it, this Smriti simply declines to extend its own sanction, exactly as it declines to sanction any other practice that fails the threefold test (§4.7, on the texts as final arbiter, applies the same logic to community disputes generally).

This Smriti does not ask a practitioner to take this conclusion on faith. It asks the same thing it asks of every other claim: examine the evidence honestly, reason from it plainly, and let the conclusion stand or fall on that basis rather than on sentiment, family pressure, or the comfort of an existing custom. A community genuinely committed to non-harm cannot claim that commitment while remaining willfully uninformed about a documented and preventable source of harm to children. This is Ajñānam (§4.9) in one of its most consequential forms, and this Smriti declines to enable it.

Where a couple believes their specific degree of relation falls outside genuinely elevated risk, a matter properly assessed by genetic counseling rather than by community opinion, the community may, at its discretion, ask for that assessment before proceeding. Where there is doubt, the presumption favors caution and the child's welfare, not the couple's preference.

The ceremony (Vivāha Saṃskāra). Conducted in the presence of community, led by an elder or mutually respected practitioner, never a priest by hereditary right.

  1. Agni (Sacred Fire). A small fire is kindled as witness to the vows (or a lamp lit, if fire is impractical). Fire represents the divine light of consciousness that never varies.

  2. Exchange of Vows. The couple exchanges vows covering the four aims of life:

  3. Dharma: "We promise to encourage each other's practice and study, and to keep the ideals of the path alive in our home."
  4. Artha: "We promise to support each other materially, to practice honest livelihood, and to share our resources generously."
  5. Kāma: "We promise to love and cherish each other, to see the divine in each other, and to use our bond as a means to soften the ego, not inflate it."
  6. Mokṣa: "We remember that our ultimate goal is liberation. We will not cling to each other in fear, but support each other's journey toward the Ātman, even as we journey together."

A simple mutual affirmation follows: "I take your hand in friendship and dharma. May our life together be an offering."

  1. Saptapadi, Seven Steps. The couple takes seven steps together, speaking an aspiration at each: nourishing each other's practice; cultivating patience and strength; prospering and sharing with others; being companions on the path to freedom; raising children in dharma, if it is to be; growing old in mutual respect; remaining friends through all seasons of life.

  2. Exchange of Symbols. Rings are exchanged, or a Mangalsūtra tied, with the words: "This is a symbol of our shared commitment, not ownership. You are always the free Ātman."

  3. Community Blessing. The community showers flower petals and chants the community mantra, perhaps with kīrtan.

  4. Feast, closing the ceremony in shared celebration.

On separation. This Smriti does not treat marriage as indissoluble by definition, nor does it treat its ending lightly. Where a marriage has genuinely broken down, through incompatibility, harm, or the honest conclusion of both partners that continuing together no longer serves either's path, the community's role is support, not judgment: the same principles of Leaving Without Drama (§4.7) and freedom from shame apply to the ending of a marriage as to any other transition. Where the breakdown involves harm, particularly to a partner or to children, the full Conflict Resolution and Protection provisions of this Book (§4.7, §4.8) apply without exception.

4.6 The Ten Rites of Passage (Saṃskāras), Conception to Death

These rites mark the significant passages of a life, adapted from the ancient Saṃskāras of Sanātana Dharma but stripped of caste distinction, priestly hierarchy, and any assumption that sanctity depends on birth. None is obligatory. Any may be performed by the practitioners themselves, by family, or by a respected community elder; none requires ordination beyond sincerity and a living connection to practice. Adapt freely, simplify, translate, shorten, the inner offering, not the exact form, is what makes any of these sacred.

4.6.1 Garbhādhāna, Consecration of Conception

When: before or at conception, for a couple consciously choosing to invite a child into their life.

How:
1. Both partners bathe, dress simply, sit together, light a lamp and incense.
2. Speak a shared intention aloud, not a demand but a humble invitation: "May this child, if it is to be, be born in good health, with a mind inclined toward Dharma. May we be worthy guardians, taking this step with full awareness, renouncing attachment to outcomes."
3. Recite the chosen Iṣṭa mantra three times together, then the community mantra for one full mālā round or as feels right.
4. Offer a flower, fruit, or sweet to the Iṣṭa Devatā. Optionally touch each other's hearts, saying "Aham Brahmāsmi", I am Brahman.
5. Sit together in silence for a few minutes; close with "Om Shantih Shantih Shantih."

This ritual does not guarantee conception nor petition for a specific outcome. If conception does not occur, the practice is to accept the unfolding as it is (Īśvara Praṇidhāna, surrender).

4.6.2 Simantonnayana, Blessing the Mother and Unborn Child

When: fourth to seventh month of pregnancy, traditionally the fourth month.

How:
1. The mother sits comfortably, perhaps adorned with flowers by family or friends.
2. Partner or an elder lights a lamp.
3. A short passage is read from the Devī Gītā or Śrīmad Bhāgavatam on the sacredness of the body and the divine presence within all beings.
4. The community chants the Iṣṭa mantra three times, then the community mantra for one round.
5. The partner or an elder marks the mother's forehead with kumkum or sandalwood paste: "May the divine Mother protect you and the child you carry. May your heart be at peace." Others may offer flowers or their own blessings.
6. The gathered community speaks its commitment aloud: "We are here with you."
7. Close with shared silence and the Shanti mantra.

4.6.3 Jātakarma, Welcoming the Newborn

When: as soon as practical after birth, ideally within the first hours or days, performed simply by the parents, needing no audience.

How:
1. Touch the baby's lips gently with a clean finger dipped in honey or ghee, and whisper into the right ear, three times: "Tat Tvam Asi", that thou art, or "Aham Brahmāsmi."
2. Softly recite the names of the family's Iṣṭa Devatā or the community mantra into the child's ear.
3. Say: "May you live long and strong. May you grow in wisdom and compassion. We are your guardians; the divine is your ultimate parent."
4. If nursing, let this be the moment of first feeding, offered as sacred nourishment.

4.6.4 Nāmakaraṇa, The Naming Ceremony

When: tenth, eleventh, or twelfth day after birth, or any auspicious day within the first few months.

How:
1. Family and community gather; the child, bathed and freshly dressed, is held by the mother or a designated elder.
2. Parents state the chosen name and its meaning aloud, names of divine forms, virtues (Śānti, Dayā, Satya), or nature (Ākāś, Pṛthvī) are encouraged, though wide latitude is given.
3. Parents lean close to the baby's right ear and speak the name three times: "You are [Name]. May your name be a blessing and a remembrance of your true self."
4. The community chants the Iṣṭa mantra three times and the community mantra one round.
5. Elders may offer a flower, touch the child's head gently: "May you walk the path of Dharma. May you be a light unto yourself."
6. Close with a shared meal.

4.6.5 Annaprāśana, First Feeding of Solid Food

When: around six months of age.

How:
1. The family sits together at mealtime; a small portion of soft rice, kheer, or fruit is prepared.
2. A lamp is lit, and the family chants: "Annam Brahma, Raso Vishnuh, Bhokta Devo Maheshvarah", food is Brahman, its essence is Vishnu, the enjoyer is the divine.
3. A parent feeds the first spoonful: "May this food nourish your body and mind. May you never go hungry. May you always share what you have with others."
4. The child is fed as usual; the family eats together in gratitude.

4.6.6 Sādhana Dīkṣā, Initiation into Practice

When: when a young person (typically 8–16, though open to any age) sincerely wishes to commit to daily practice. This replaces the traditional sacred-thread ceremony and is open equally to all genders and backgrounds.

How:
1. The initiate bathes, dresses simply, and sits before a teacher or elder recognized by the community for depth and integrity of practice, no formal lineage required.
2. The initiate speaks a clear vow: "I, [Name], of my own free will, commit to this path. I promise to practice daily to the best of my ability, to study with sincerity, to treat all beings with respect, and to seek truth above comfort."
3. The teacher whispers the Iṣṭa mantra three times into the initiate's right ear, line by line, the initiate repeating; a mālā is given for japa.
4. A simple nyāsa is performed: touching heart, shoulders, eyes, and crown, repeating "Aham Brahmāsmi" at the heart and "Tat Tvam Asi" at the crown.
5. Those present place a hand over the heart: "We support you. We walk with you. Keep practicing."
6. A copy of the Gītā is presented, marking the beginning of serious study.
7. Close with celebration.

4.6.7 Vivāha, The Household Union

See §4.5 above for the full definition and ceremony.

4.6.8 Vānaprastha Āśramaṇa, Entering the Forest of Contemplation

When: when children are grown and one feels called to turn more deeply inward, typically 50–60 and beyond, but a matter of inner readiness, not a fixed number.

How:
1. The individual or couple sits with family, expresses gratitude for the householder years, and asks for blessings to simplify life and deepen practice.
2. A symbolic gift is offered to children or community, books, a small donation, a meaningful object, signifying the transfer of active responsibility.
3. A vow of simplicity is spoken: "I now turn more fully inward. Our home is open as a place of Satsaṅga. We release the need to control outcomes."
4. Family and community chant the Iṣṭa and community mantras, and an elder may perform nyāsa on the couple, touching heart and crown, saying "Tat Tvam Asi."
5. There is no change in official title; the person remains a householder with renewed dedication, wearing simple clothing only if it feels natural.

4.6.9 Sannyāsa Dīkṣā, Full Renunciation (Rare, Optional)

When: very rarely, when a practitioner feels an unmistakable call to complete renunciation of possessions, family ties, and social identity. Not the path of the great majority, who complete the journey as householders.

How:
1. The individual gives away all possessions and performs their own symbolic funeral rites, the symbolic death of the ego.
2. They receive the Mahāvākyas as their only "possession," and take vows of extreme non-harming, celibacy, non-possession, and truthfulness, keeping perhaps a single cloth and a begging bowl.
3. The community chants "Om Tat Sat" and releases them: "Go, and may you realize the Self in this very life."
4. They do not return to their old life, and the community does not track them.

4.6.10 Antyeṣṭi, The Final Rite

When: at death, conducted by family and community, ideally with someone able to hold the space without fear.

How:
1. As death approaches, family and close practitioners gather, chanting softly, without wailing. The dying person is gently reminded of their practice and the nature of the Ātman. A lamp stays lit.
2. After death, the body is bathed and dressed simply; sacred ash or sandalwood paste is applied to the forehead.
3. The community carries the body to the cremation ground (or crematorium), chanting the community mantra or "Rama, Rama."
4. A small fire is kindled where possible (modern crematorium adaptations are acceptable, the intent matters, not the exact wood-fire form). The chief mourner recites: "This body, made of the five elements, returns to the elements. The Ātman was never born; it does not die. Aham Brahmāsmi." Ghee and camphor are offered into the fire while the community's opening and main kīrtan-mantras are chanted.
5. Afterward, the community gathers for a short Satsaṅga; a passage on the immortality of the soul (Gītā Chapter 2, or the Kaṭha Upaniṣad's account of Naciketā) is read.
6. There is no fixed period of ritual impurity. Family may gather on the third, tenth, or twelfth day to share food and memory. Ashes may be scattered in a river, ocean, or sacred place: "May you be free."
7. The family continues its practice; the community rallies around them. Death is treated as a transition, not an ending, and the person's life is celebrated as inspiration.

4.7 Conflict Resolution

Conflicts will arise. They are not signs of failure; they are opportunities for practice. The community handles them grounded in Ahiṃsā, Satya, and Kṣamā.

  1. Direct communication. Speak privately and respectfully to the person involved first, in the spirit of "I want to understand and resolve this." Do not gather allies or spread the grievance before this step.
  2. Witnessed conversation. If direct communication fails or you feel unsafe, request a mutually trusted third party, a Serious Practitioner or elder, to facilitate. Any party has the right to bring a trusted companion as a silent witness at any stage.
  3. Community circle. For serious breaches (manipulation, exploitation, abuse, chronic harm), a small group of elders and respected members convenes a circle. Both parties are heard. The aim is restoration and protection, not punishment.
  4. Separation. If severe harm is established and the person shows no genuine remorse or willingness to change, they may be asked to leave, transparently, with explanation to the community, as a last resort. The door remains open only if genuine change becomes evident.
  5. No shunning without process. No member is ignored, exiled, or ostracized without a fair, transparent process.
  6. No retaliation. No one who raises a good-faith concern may be retaliated against, shunned, or punished.
  7. Right to appeal. Any party may request review by a larger group of previously uninvolved Serious Practitioners if a decision feels unjust.
  8. Transparency of serious outcomes. If a member is asked to leave due to severe misconduct, the community is informed of the reason in general terms, balancing honesty with the privacy and dignity of all involved. The purpose is to protect the community, not to shame.

The texts as final arbiter. When disputes arise over teaching, conduct, or direction, we turn to the source texts, the Gītā, Upaniṣads, Yoga Vāsiṣṭha, and the rest, as common ground. Any interpretation must be tested against the Threefold Test (§1.3). No individual's claim overrides this test. If a Serious Practitioner or elder persistently teaches something contrary to the core texts and principles of Advaita Vedānta as this community understands them, the community, through its collective discernment, may ask them to stop teaching until the matter is clarified.

Leaving the community. Leaving is as sacred as arriving. People leave for many reasons, life circumstances, changes in belief, a different path calling. There is no guilt, no drama, no shaming. Simply inform a Gathering Facilitator if you wish, or just stop attending; you are always welcome to return. If you leave due to conflict or hurt, this process remains available. There is no "excommunication" for doubt or disagreement, honest questioning is a sign of a healthy mind. A community that cannot tolerate sincere dissent is a sect, not a Satsaṅga.

4.8 Checks and Balances: Guarding Against the Corruption of Power

Every spiritual community, however sincere at its start, is vulnerable to the ego's tendency to seek control, comfort, and reputation even while speaking eloquently of non-duality. History offers countless warnings: communities that began with genuine insight have calcified into sects, surrendered their moral compass to charismatic figures, or quietly normalized behaviors that betray their own founding ideals. These structures exist not from distrust, but as an expression of the very values this path teaches: Ahiṃsā, Satya, Damyata, and accountability to the texts.

No Single Point of Power

  • Leadership rests with a Council of Elders of at least three people (or, in a small community, open discussion among all Serious Practitioners); no major financial, ethical, or directional decision is made by one individual.
  • Functional roles, Gathering Facilitator, Study Coordinator, Kīrtan Lead, rotate at least yearly, without a conscious community reaffirmation. This prevents the natural human tendency to equate a function with a permanent status.
  • Any elder may be asked to step back from active decision-making for a period if concerns about their conduct are raised and validated through the conflict process, a protection, not a punishment.
  • No role is ever passed to a child or relative simply by family connection.

Financial Transparency

  • All funds are recorded in an open ledger, reviewable by any member on request, reviewed openly at least once a year.
  • No personal profit from teaching, initiation, or ritual; any financial support an elder needs to dedicate time to community service must be unconditional, transparent, community-consented dāna (gift), never a fee.
  • Community and personal finances are kept entirely separate.
  • No pressure to give, no solicitation through guilt, promised spiritual benefit, or social pressure. Fundraising, where it occurs, is for clearly stated, concrete needs, conducted with restraint.

Accountability in Teaching

Every teaching passes the Threefold Test (§1.3). Teachers remain open to peer feedback from a small study circle rather than teaching unchallenged. There are no hidden or "higher" teachings and no tiered system of spiritual secrecy, the texts are open, the practices are described, and any Serious Practitioner may receive personal guidance, but the core of the path is transparent and equal for all. Any member, at any level, has the right to respectfully question a teaching or decision; this is Vichāra, not insubordination.

Ethical Boundaries for Elders

An elder must not enter a romantic relationship with someone they actively guide (§4.3); must never encourage a practitioner to cut ties with family or friends to "focus on the path" or receive "special guidance", isolation is a classic sign of undue influence; must report ethical misconduct they become aware of, especially by another elder, since silence in the face of harm is complicity; and is encouraged toward an honest yearly self-audit: Am I becoming attached to my role? Am I seeking validation? Have I caused harm? Am I practicing what I teach?

Protection of the Vulnerable

  • Any gathering involving children requires at least two adults present; no adult works alone with children in a secluded setting. This is simple and non-negotiable.
  • Any suspicion of abuse of a minor is reported to the council and to civil authorities, the community never shields an abuser under cover of "internal resolution." The safety of the child is paramount.
  • The community is not a substitute for professional mental health care; elders gently encourage professional help alongside practice when distress is severe.
  • A practitioner growing unhealthily dependent on an elder is met with special care, the elder actively redirects them back toward their own practice and the wider community, never fostering exclusive reliance. Devotion (guru-bhakti) in this community is directed toward the texts and the divine, never toward a single human being in a way that creates unhealthy attachment.

Against Doctrinal Rigidity

Honest doubt is the engine of inquiry, not a sign of weak faith. No metaphysical creed is required for belonging, a practitioner who struggles with a particular doctrine is not thereby less of a practitioner; the measure is practice and conduct. The community actively welcomes diversity of background and periodically invites an outside scholar or practitioner from another lineage to offer a mirror against blind spots, an external perspective can reveal what internal familiarity obscures.

Naming the Warning Signs of Cult Formation

Explicitly, so they can be recognized: a single leader or clique becoming unquestionable; members afraid to leave; increasing isolation from outside relationships; financial or sexual exploitation; suppression of dissent or labeling critics as "impure"; a grandiose sense of being the one true path. If these appear, the community commits to immediate open discussion, external consultation, and willingness to radically restructure or dissolve existing leadership, because the survival of the community is never the highest value; the integrity of the path is.

Dissolution

If a significant number of Serious Practitioners believe the community no longer serves Dharma, a general gathering may be called to discuss dissolution, requiring a two-thirds majority after a period of reflection. Remaining assets go to a charitable or dharmic cause chosen by consensus; no individual profits. Dissolution is not failure, the teachings do not die with the community; they live wherever sincere seekers practice them.

4.9 Guarding the Inner Life: The Demonic Tendencies and Their Antidotes

The greatest threat to any spiritual community does not come from outside. It comes from within, from the human tendencies the Bhagavad Gītā names Āsuri Sampadā, the demonic endowments (introduced in Book Two, §2.6). These are not the traits of mythical monsters. They are the daily furniture of the unexamined ego, present in every practitioner, capable of arising even in those who speak eloquently of non-duality. A community that fails to recognize and guard against them will, in time, become the very thing it set out to transcend.

From the Vidura Nīti, the six enemies (Ṣaḍripu) operating within every heart: kāma, krodha, lobha, moha, mada, matsarya, desire, anger, greed, delusion, pride, and jealousy. Jealousy (matsarya) is particularly toxic in community life: the silent resentment of another's practice or recognition, leading to subtle undermining and gossip.

This is not an external enemy to be defeated. It is the texture of the ego itself. The defence is recognition, restraint, and transformation, not extermination.

Applying the Threefold Test to Inner States

When a strong impulse arises, anger at a community member who criticized you, apply the same test used for teachings (§1.3): Śabda, do the texts validate this reaction? The Gītā describes anger as a direct path to the loss of discrimination and ruin; the Sthitaprajña's mark is freedom from anger. Yukti, does this anger make sense? Is the perceived threat real, or a threat to the ego's image? Anubhūti, in the quiet of meditation, when the mind settles, does this anger still feel true, or does it dissolve like a cloud in the sky of awareness?

This simple, repeated inquiry creates a gap between the impulse and identification with it. The practitioner learns to say "anger is present" rather than "I am angry." This shift, from identification to observation, is the core of the inner defence.

The mirror of Satsaṅga. We cannot see our own blind spots by definition; the Āsuri qualities are particularly skilled at hiding from their host. A trusted companion who says, "I notice you become sharp when your teaching is questioned," is offering a priceless gift. A practitioner who refuses all such feedback has already succumbed to Darpa.

The litmus question. Does this thought, word, or action lead toward greater clarity, compassion, and freedom from the ego's grip, or toward greater confusion, separation, and self-importance? Applied sincerely in the moment, this often reveals an impulse's nature before it is acted upon, a form of instant Vichāra.

Ātma-anveṣaṇa as the root defence. All Āsuri qualities are expressions of the ego's fundamental insecurity, desperate strategies of a self that believes it is separate, mortal, and under threat. The ultimate personal defence is the inquiry (§3.5) into the nature of the "I" that feels threatened, proud, angry, or jealous. As the inquiry deepens and the practitioner begins to taste the truth of the Ātman, already whole, already free, untouched by praise or blame, the grip of the Āsuri Sampadā loosens naturally. This is not overnight; it is the work of a lifetime, but every moment of genuine inquiry weakens the demonic tendency and strengthens the divine.

The daily recalibration. The formal practice sequence (Book Three) is itself a powerful defence. A practitioner who maintains this daily rhythm is far less likely to be swept away by Āsuri impulses, because the entire system is regularly recalibrated toward sattva. Neglect the daily practice, and the door opens for old patterns to reassert themselves.

Community-Level Defences

A culture of honest feedback, without cruelty. A community where no one dares give honest feedback to an elder is ripe for Dambha and Darpa. Feedback must be timely (addressed when fresh, not stored as ammunition), specific ("in yesterday's discussion, when I raised a question, you dismissed it with a joke"), kind in intent (aimed at growth, not shaming), and received with openness (the receiver practicing Kṣamā and Hrīḥ, listening without immediate defensiveness).

The Council of Elders as an accountability body. Elders must meet regularly not only to manage logistics but to hold a mirror to each other: where have I noticed Dambha or Darpa in myself this month? Have I treated anyone with subtle cruelty or neglect? Have I used my position to avoid accountability? Is there anyone in the community I am avoiding or resenting? An elder who refuses this process is already in the grip of Ajñānam.

Protecting the vulnerable from spiritual abuse. Pāruṣya in a spiritual community often wears the mask of "tough teaching" or "cutting through the ego." A teacher or elder who consistently belittles, humiliates, isolates, or psychologically pressures a practitioner, even in the language of Advaita, is manifesting Āsuri Sampadā, not wisdom. A single instance of genuine cruelty, unrepented and unreformed, is grounds for immediate removal from any position of influence.

Preventing the cult of personality. The Āsuri Sampadā finds its most fertile ground in the cult of the charismatic individual. Rotating roles, no single leader, no hereditary authority, no unquestionable guru (§4.8) are direct defences. Icons attract Dambha; icons also fall. When the community notices excessive deference toward one individual, it is the community's duty to gently and publicly rebalance.

Transparency as a disinfectant. Secrecy is the oxygen of the Āsuri Sampadā. Financial opacity, closed-door decisions, whispered hierarchies, and "special" inner circles all create conditions where these qualities thrive.

The collective Vichāra. Periodically, the community should dedicate a gathering to collective self-examination: have we, as a community, exhibited any of these tendencies? Is there a quality particularly strong right now? What structures might be feeding it? This is not public shaming; it is sober, honest, compassionate group inquiry, Satsaṅga at its most real.

When It Takes Root and Will Not Yield

A pattern of dismissive or manipulative behavior; consistent refusal to acknowledge harm; gathering uncritical supporters while isolating or discrediting critics; using spiritual language to justify unethical conduct, these call for the full conflict-resolution process (§4.7), up to and including separation. Tolerating unrepentant Āsuri Sampadā in the name of "non-judgment" or "inclusivity" is itself a betrayal of the values this community exists to uphold. The Gītā (16.21) is blunt: these three gates lead to ruin. The community must have the courage to close them when necessary.

The Ultimate Defence: Surrender to the Divine

At the deepest level, the Āsuri Sampadā is the ego's attempt to be its own master, its own god. The Daivī Sampadā is the natural blossoming of a life oriented toward something larger than the small self, and the Gītā's teaching is that even the sattvic ego is transcended through ananya-bhakti (Book Two, §2.1, §2.7), undivided devotion.

For the practitioner struggling with pride, anger, jealousy, or willful blindness, the most powerful medicine is often the simplest: lay it at the feet of the Iṣṭa. Offer the ugly impulse as worship. "Here, O Divine, is my jealousy. I cannot conquer it. Take it. Transform it into love." This is not a bypass; it is an acknowledgment of the ego's limits and an opening to grace, precisely what Ātma-bhāvita Bhakti (§2.3) makes available.

The community's shared mantra, Kālī-Durge Rādheśyām, Gaurīśaṅkar Sītārām, holds within it all the divine forms named in Book One: Kālī, who dissolves the ego's pretensions; Durgā, who protects the vulnerable from harm; Rādhe-Śyām, the love that melts pride; Gaurī-Śaṅkar, the union of Śakti and Śiva; Sītā-Rām, dharma lived in a life. To chant it with full heart, especially in moments of inner darkness, is to invite divine presence into the very chambers the Āsuri qualities seek to occupy.

4.10 The Community and the Wider World

We do not proselytize and do not disparage other genuine paths, hereditary or otherwise. This community exists for those who have no other home; it is not a competitor to those who already have one. We treat all Hindu temples, traditions, and communities with respect, even those that would not recognize us, we do not claim to represent Hinduism, nor do we seek recognition from any Hindu authority. If a member wishes to also participate in a traditional Hindu temple or community, they are encouraged to do so; our path is not exclusive.

When faced with dogmatic pressure or hostility from outside, a demand for blind belief, condemnation, or attempts to disrupt gatherings, the response rests on three pillars:

Epistemic. State plainly that this path tests any claim, including its own, against reason and experience (§1.3), and that a truth which fears questioning is not truth, without needing to argue against anyone else's revelation directly. The tradition distinguishes Śraddhā (provisional trust that practice will yield direct knowing) from blind faith (a permanent suspension of inquiry, which the Gītā names as a form of Ajñānam). When pressed, the final stance is simple: "You are free to believe as you choose. We are free to inquire as we must. Let our respective lives demonstrate which path bears the fruit of wisdom and peace." A system that rejects reason as a valid means of knowledge cannot be engaged in rational debate, this is recognized rather than fought, and the response is to bear witness through the visible fruits of steadiness, compassion, and clarity rather than argument.

Personal. Cultivate fearlessness (Abhayam, the recognition that the Ātman cannot be harmed by any external verdict, not the absence of fear but the refusal to let fear determine choices); steadiness under praise or blame (pausing before responding, declining to accept an attacker's frame, recommitting to daily practice as an anchor); compassion without appeasement (holding "this person is suffering; may they find peace; I need neither convince them nor submit to them", firm and kind, like a tree that bends in wind but stays rooted); and the willingness to use silence (mauna) rather than argue with someone determined to misunderstand, not as cowardice but as the wisdom of conserving energy for practice and genuine Satsaṅga. Reach out to community rather than facing pressure alone, isolation is where such pressure does its worst damage, and Satsaṅga is its most direct antidote.

Social. Never retaliate or mirror an attacker's methods, the moment this community begins to mirror propaganda, demonization, or calls for loyalty against a common enemy, it has become what it stands against. Correct misinformation with calm, factual, non-escalating statements, and let truth stand on its own rather than chasing every falsehood. Protect vulnerable members facing family or social pressure without ever encouraging severed family ties unless those ties are genuinely abusive. Maintain boundaries at gatherings calmly and privately, disruptive individuals are asked to respect the space, and asked to leave if they do not, without public shaming. Pursue legal recourse only as a last resort, for genuine legal harm, undertaken soberly and with full community consultation. And take the long view: this path does not need to win every exchange, only to keep sincere practice alive. Those drawn to it will find it; those who attack it will pass. The tree does not uproot itself because the wind blows.

CLOSING

5.1 On Adapting This Smriti

Amendments to this Smriti may be proposed by any Serious Practitioner and are discussed openly at gatherings; changes are adopted by broad consensus, never unilateral decree. This document is a living one, meant to be revised as the community learns.

5.2 On Holding It Lightly

Every teaching in Vidyā Khaṇḍa, every union described in Yoga Khaṇḍa, every practice in Kriyā Khaṇḍa, every rite and safeguard in Ācāra Khaṇḍa is a shell. Without sincerity these are empty forms; with sincerity they become genuine vehicles for a truth that needed no vehicle to begin with. Use this Smriti the way one uses a boat to cross a river, with full attention while crossing, and without any need to keep carrying it once the far shore is reached.

5.3 The Recognition Beneath All of It

Whichever of the five streams calls to your heart most naturally, the stillness of Śiva, the sustaining love of Viṣṇu and his forms, the fierce and tender power of the Goddess, the cheerful obstacle-clearing wisdom of Gaṇeśa, or the constant visible light of the Sun, every rite, every daily sitting, every safeguard in this manual exists only to carry you back to what was never actually absent:

Tat Tvam Asi. That thou art.
