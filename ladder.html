Saptabhūmi: The Seven Stations

The Ladder

Seven stations from first curiosity to direct recognition, showing how philosophical understanding, Aanvikshiki inquiry, simplified Agamic practice, Seva, and the three qualities of Dama, Daya, and Daan deepen together, rung by rung, into the recognition that the ladder was always standing on what it was climbing toward.

Dama Self-Restraint

Voluntary mastery of sense-impulses. Grows from a single daily pause into a settled freedom from compulsion.

Daya Compassion

Felt care for the reality of another being. Grows from full attention to one person into the spontaneous recognition of the same Self in all.

Daan Generosity

Giving that goes before expectation. Grows from one genuine act into a life in which the giving and the living are one gesture.

Seva Service

Unconditional service to the world through genuine nature, skill, tendency, and life-station. The primary external sadhana.

Mind Understanding

Philosophical and epistemological depth: Vedanta for the ultimate, Aanvikshiki and Nyaya-Vaisheshika for the empirical.

The bars show depth, not time. Some practitioners spend years at a rung. A few move faster. Neither is the measure. Begin at whatever rung honest self-assessment places you and use the rung above as a compass, not a verdict.

1

Sparsha

First Touch

Entry: Curiosity only. No philosophy, no prior practice, no existing belief required. One genuine question is enough.

Mind and Inquiry

No concepts required. One honest question lived rather than answered: "What actually am I?" Introduce the four Nyaya pramanas at their simplest: I trust what I directly perceive, what I can soundly infer, what careful analogy reveals, and what reliable persons who have looked closely report. Apply this standard to one claim today.

Practice

5 minutes of sitting still each morning. Eyes closed. No technique. Light a lamp before sitting; sit before it for a moment and simply note that the lamp illuminates without effort or preference. Once during the day ask: "Who is aware of what I am thinking right now?" Do not answer. Let the question settle.

Seva

One genuine act of service per week through whatever role or skill you actually have. A teacher teaches one student who cannot pay. A cook prepares one meal for a neighbor in need. A builder fixes something for someone who cannot fix it alone. Anonymous is better. No announcement.

Bhakti

Light a lamp for any of the five forms that draws you. Sit before it for two minutes. Offer nothing yet. Simply attend. The form is a symbol of Brahman. Sit as if you are in the presence of what you are looking for, because you are.

2

Shubhecchā

Good Desire

Entry: A genuine, recurring pull toward the path. Not intellectual interest but a lived longing for something beyond what ordinary seeking has delivered.

Mind and Inquiry

Read "Who Am I?" by Ramana Maharshi. Understand one thing clearly: you can be aware of thoughts, feelings, and sensations, which means the one who is aware of them cannot be any of them. The witness is not what is witnessed. Learn the four Nyaya pramanas by name and begin consciously applying them: when you hear a claim, ask which pramana it rests on.

Practice

20-minute morning sitting: 5 min of breath settling, simple puja to your chosen Ishta-devata from the five forms with one mantra and one offering of water or a flower, 10 min of japa with a mala, 5 min of sitting in silence after. Evening: one honest sentence about the day. "Where today did I act from fear or craving rather than clarity?"

Seva

Name your seva: the specific unconditional service you offer through your genuine nature and skill. Commit to it once a week, consistently. Before beginning, offer it to the Whole. After completing it, release the result. Do not track whether gratitude was expressed. This is the beginning of karma yoga as a lived practice.

Bhakti

Weekly kirtan or devotional prayer. Your chosen Ishta-devata from the Pancha-devata is approached daily with a single mantra. Begin learning what this form symbolizes so the devotion has understanding behind it. Devotion without understanding tends toward superstition; understanding without devotion tends toward dryness.

3

Vichāraṇā

Inquiry

Entry: A sustained daily practice for at least three months. Study has begun. The inquiry is no longer occasional curiosity but an active, daily movement of attention.

Mind and Inquiry

Read Tattvabodha. Understand the three bodies, five sheaths, three states of experience, and the Mahavakyas by name and meaning. Know what Brahman and Atman mean technically, not just spiritually. Learn the Nyaya five-membered syllogism: statement, reason, example, application, conclusion. Practice constructing one valid argument per week and identifying the fallacy (hetvabhasa) in one claim you encounter. Introduction to the Vaisheshika categories of the natural world.

Practice

30 to 45 minute morning sitting: 10 rounds of Nadi Shodhana, Pancha-devata puja to all five forms with Panchopachara (five offerings: fragrance, flower, incense, light, and water), japa, self-inquiry. 15 minutes of Svadhyaya daily, reading one passage from a named text and applying Aanvikshiki: what is the claim, what is the reasoning, what is the evidence, what objections arise and are they well-answered.

Seva

Weekly seva in your natural domain, with conscious Ishvara-arpana (offering to the Whole) before beginning and deliberate release of the result after. Extend it: if your skill is knowledge, teach it freely to one person who cannot access it otherwise. If your skill is physical work, do one project for someone who needs it. If your skill is care, give one sustained hour of it to someone who is not in your immediate circle.

Bhakti

Kirtan or devotional practice weekly, either alone or with others. The symbolism of your chosen Ishta-devata is now understood well enough that the puja has genuine content: you know what you are approaching and why this form. The devotion begins to feel genuine rather than performed. Learning the Advaitic understanding of bhakti: the devotee and the Beloved share the same ultimate ground.

4

Tanumanāsā

Thinning of the Mind

Entry: Practice is embedded in daily life. Study has real depth. The mind begins to quiet on its own, without effort to quiet it. Equanimity shows up unsummoned in small situations.

Mind and Inquiry

Read Panchadashi. Understand Maya and Avidya as the veiling and projecting functions of ignorance. Be able to explain mithya without collapsing it to "the world is fake." Understand adhyaropa-apavada as a teaching method. Read Shankara's Gita Bhashya on karma yoga. Begin the Upanishads directly with commentary. Nyaya fallacies (hetvabhasa) now recognized in real arguments: ad hominem, circular reasoning, false equivalence. Vaisheshika categories applied to understanding the physical world in the spirit of modern science.

Practice

60-minute morning sitting. Pancha-devata puja now uses the full Panchopachara with brief meditation on the symbolism of each form before the offering. Karma yoga is the conscious operating frame for all professional and domestic work: not as an idea but as an actual inner practice before and after significant actions. Svadhyaya deepens into the Upanishads with Shankara's commentary. Satsanga becomes a genuine transmission field.

Seva

Seva begins to reorganize life. No longer one discrete hour per week but a current running through the professional and domestic life. The skill offered deepens: what is given is the best of what is available, not the surplus. A teacher who reaches this rung restructures how they teach so that the quality of what they offer becomes genuinely excellent. A healer gives more fully. A craftsperson produces with greater integrity. The seva is becoming the primary outer life.

Bhakti

Daily devotional practice. The Ishta-devata relationship has genuine feeling and genuine understanding simultaneously. The form is loved and the meaning is clear. The practitioner understands, from reading and reflection, how the greatest bhaktas arrived at non-dual recognition through the most intense personal devotion. They begin to feel, even briefly, that the devotee and the Beloved are not as separate as they seemed.

5

Sattvāpatti

Attainment of Clarity

Entry: Equanimity is no longer an achievement. It is the default register. The formal practice and the rest of the day have begun to merge. The three qualities are present without effort in most situations.

Mind and Inquiry

Able to sit with the Brahma Sutras with Shankara's commentary. Understands adhyaropa-apavada as an operating lens, not just a concept. Sees through both the "world is solid and final" error and the "world is nothing" error simultaneously. Can hold paradox without discomfort. The relationship between Nyaya-Vaisheshika and Vedanta is now transparent: two tools for two levels, neither canceling the other. Empirical claims are met with rigorous Aanvikshiki. Ultimate questions are met with Vedantic inquiry. No confusion between levels.

Practice

The formal sitting and the rest of the day are no longer sharply distinct. Chup-Sadhana is the background hum throughout ordinary activity. Self-inquiry operates throughout, not only on the cushion. The Pancha-devata puja continues daily but has become a different act: the understanding that the form is Brahman is now steady rather than occasional, so the puja is genuinely an offering to the Self. The Satsanga has become a field of real transmission.

Seva

Life is the seva. The distinction between professional life and service practice has dissolved. What is done is what serves, and what serves is what is done. The skill brought to the world is the fullest expression of what this person actually is: not performed for an audience, not withheld for advantage, not diluted by self-concern. The teacher teaches with everything available. The healer heals with everything available. The builder builds with everything available.

Bhakti

The devotee and the Beloved are felt as not-two, not as a philosophical conclusion but as a direct experience that arises in the puja, in kirtan, and in moments of stillness. Love without a fixed object. The puja continues, with genuine feeling, but the understanding beneath it has shifted: what is being loved is what one already is.

6

Asaṃsakti

Non-Attachment

Entry: Nothing clings and nothing is avoided. The world is fully engaged with, fully cared for, and not held. This rung is lived before it is described.

Mind and Inquiry

The texts are companions, not maps, because the territory is now known directly. Can transmit the teaching clearly to another person who is ready. Knows precisely what cannot be said and does not say it. Nyaya-Vaisheshika and Vedanta are used with complete transparency about which level each addresses, and without needing to defend the distinction because it has become obvious. The entire philosophical architecture serves as a precision instrument for pointing at what is already recognized.

Practice

The sitting and the life are one unbroken practice. Work, relationships, service, and study all arise within the same steadiness that the morning sitting once produced for an hour. Satsanga produces genuine transmission outward, not as performance of attainment but as the natural expression of what has been recognized. The Pancha-devata puja may continue or may be understood as continuous: the world itself has become the altar and every encounter an offering.

Seva

The whole of one's being is an offering. There is no distinction between living and giving. The seva does not feel like a practice anymore; it feels like breathing. The form it takes is still shaped by genuine nature and genuine skill, but the unconditional quality is now completely natural rather than cultivated. There is no residue of tracking, no need for acknowledgment, and no withholding for personal advantage. What is available flows to where it is needed.

Bhakti

Love as the nature of awareness itself, not as a practice, not as an emotion, and not as a relationship between two separate beings. The Beloved and the lover have been recognized as one movement. The puja may continue as an expression of this love, but the understanding that grounds it is now the ground itself rather than a concept held by a practitioner.

7

Turīya

The Fourth

Not a rung reached by climbing. A recognition of what the ladder was always standing on.

Recognition

The teaching has dissolved into the one it was pointing at. Not more philosophy: less. Not accumulation: subtraction. The knower, the known, and the knowing are seen as one movement. Nyaya-Vaisheshika continues to describe the empirical world accurately. It describes a world arising within the same awareness that is now recognized directly. There is no conflict and no choice to make between them.

Practice and Seva

There is no separate practice and no separate seva. The doing and the not-doing are both the same awareness. The sitting happens. The service happens. The speech happens. The silence happens. None of it produces anything that was not already present. None of it binds. What is called Dama, Daya, and Daan arises as the natural signature of a being that no longer defends a separate self against the world.

The ladder is for the one who believes they are at the bottom. Once the top is reached, it is seen that there was never a bottom or a top, and that the one who climbed was never bound. The rungs were the teaching. The recognition needs no rung.
