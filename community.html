Satsaṅga

The Circle of Seekers

A voluntary circle of practitioners. No single guru, no secret hierarchy, no pressure to stay, and no shame in leaving. We gather to study, chant, sit in silence, and hold each other accountable to the highest ideals the sages described.

What the Circle Is

The heart of this community is Satsaṅga: not a social club, not a support group, not a platform for personal ambition. We gather to practice, study, and hold each other to the path. Social warmth is welcome and natural; it is secondary to the practice. We speak to each other with honesty and care. We do not elevate any individual to the status of unquestionable authority. Teachers and elders are respected for their practice and wisdom, not worshipped, and all are subject to the same standards.

Six Principles

Voluntary participation. No one is recruited, pressured, or manipulated into joining or staying. No birth‑based hierarchy. Spiritual maturity is shown through practice and conduct, never assumed from caste, gender, or background. Accountability to texts. Every teaching answers to the Threefold Test: scripture, reason, and direct experience. Honesty and transparency. Financial arrangements, power dynamics, and personal agendas are never hidden behind spiritual language. Non‑harm. Physical, emotional, and psychological safety are actively protected. Respect for the living tradition. Hereditary Hindu communities and all genuine paths are never disparaged; this circle is a home for those who have none, not a competitor to those who already have one.

Levels of Engagement

Inquirer (Jijñāsu). Exploring freely: attending open gatherings, asking questions, coming and going with no commitment beyond basic respect for the space and the people in it. No ceremony, no declaration required.

New Practitioner (Sādhaka). Has read the invitation and feels genuine resonance. Begins a daily practice, however simple. Attends gatherings with some regularity. Declared by simply stating the intention to a facilitator or within a gathering; no formal ceremony required, though a simple blessing may be requested.

Serious Practitioner (Dṛḍha Sādhaka). Has walked the path a year or more, with practice as a central pillar of life. Requests a conversation with at least two elders: a mutual discernment, not a test. May then choose the Sādhana Dīkṣā ceremony as a public affirmation of a path already walked.

Elder / Facilitator (Mārgadarśaka). A function, not a rank. Arises from long practice, deep study, and natural aptitude. Subject to the same code as everyone else. Immediately removed from the role if the position is used for personal gain, sexual exploitation, or psychological control. The community never tolerates the emergence of a cult figure.

Who This Is For

If you feel a genuine pull toward the ancient wisdom traditions, if the portrait of the person of steady wisdom (Sthitaprajña) reads like a description of who you want to become, and if you are willing to walk the path with honesty and patience, you are welcome here. The only qualification the tradition itself ever named is sincerity.

What to Expect

Honest feedback given as an act of service. Study of the source texts without simplification. Shared sitting in silence. Kīrtan. A community that names its own shadows clearly, through the lens of the Āsurī Sampada, and works actively against them. A circle where you can ask the hardest questions without being asked to stop asking. And people who are building something together, not following something finished.

Come freely. Build together. Stay as long as the call resonates. Leave without guilt. The roar is an invitation, and a foundation stone.
