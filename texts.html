Primary Sources

The Texts We Draw From

Our primary sources span three traditions: Vedantic (philosophy and path), Agamic (devotion and ritual), and Nyaya-Vaisheshika (epistemology and natural philosophy). Each tradition operates at its own level and serves its own purpose. Together they cover the full range of what a sincere practitioner needs: understanding of what is ultimately real, tools for engaging the empirical world honestly, and a ritual form that keeps the heart alive through the long practice.

The Garjanaa Document

The Garjanaa Document is this community's complete internal guide: the full Advaita Vedanta philosophy (35 chapters), the Garjanaa Smriti (four books), the four upayas, the Bhairavi and Tattva circles, rites of passage, and all appendices, organized into four Khandas in one reference text.

Vidya, Yoga, Kriya, Charya
Vidya Khanda: Soteriology, axiology, Aanvikshiki epistemology, full Advaita ontology, Pancha-devata, and the four upayas. Yoga Khanda: The four yogas, Raja Yoga in full, qualifications, chitta-shuddhi, naishkarmya-siddhi, Manonasha, Vasanakshaya, Tattvajnana, self-inquiry, jivanmukti. Kriya Khanda: Daily practice framework, seva, Bhairavi and Tattva circles, all ten practices in detail. Vedantic Texts

Bhagavad Gita

The ethical and psychological backbone of the entire path. Contains the portraits of the Sthitaprajna (person of steady wisdom) and the mature devotee, the complete teaching of Karma Yoga, the Daivi and Asuri Sampada, and Krishna's direct declaration that all of this is Brahman. Begin here if you are new to this tradition.

Yoga Vasishtha

A vast dialogue on the nature of consciousness, liberation, and the seven stages of awakening, taught by the sage Vasishtha to Rama. Particularly valued for its psychological precision and its account of the Jivanmukta, one liberated while fully alive. The four gatekeepers of liberation it names, tranquility, contentment, self-inquiry, and the company of the wise, form a practical checklist. The triple path of Manonasha, Vasanakshaya, and Tattvajnana is drawn from this text.

Tripura Rahasya

An Advaita teaching in a devotional frame centered on the Divine Mother. Demonstrates how sincere devotion, followed to its natural depth, converges completely with direct self-knowledge. Valuable for those drawn to the integration of bhakti and jnana.

The Upanishads

The philosophical source of Advaita. The Mandukya (the three states and Turiya), Brihadaranyaka (the Mahavakyas and the nature of Brahman), Chandogya (Tat Tvam Asi), and Kena (the nature of pure awareness) are central. The Advaita guide works through all of these in careful detail with Shankara's commentary.

Ramayana and Mahabharata

Dharma lived as a life, and dharma in all its complexity. The Ramayana offers Rama's sixteen qualities as a precise portrait of realized character in the world. The Mahabharata contains the Vidura Niti, the Sanatsujatiya, and the Bhagavad Gita itself, along with countless teachings on right conduct where simple answers are never enough.

Srimad Bhagavatam and Devi Bhagavatam

The devotional heart of the tradition. The Bhagavatam's saints demonstrate that sincere practice shows up in daily conduct, not private experience. The Devi Bhagavatam contains the Goddess's own non-dual teaching, affirming that the Shakta path arrives at the same recognition as the jnana path.

Ashtavakra Gita

The most uncompromising text in the tradition, pointing at liberation immediately. Its opening verse distills the entire path: cultivate forgiveness, straightforwardness, compassion, contentment, and truthfulness as nectar. These five dissolve the ego's grip precisely because each removes a specific mechanism the ego runs on.

Agamic Texts

The Agamas

The scriptural foundation of temple worship, ritual, mantra, and the Pancha-devata practice. The Shaiva, Vaishnava, and Shakta Agamas ground devotional practice in a living, tested tradition. Our simplified daily puja draws on the principles of Panchopachara (five-fold offering) found throughout the Agamic literature, adapted for practice without a human guru or formal initiation.

Mahanirvana Tantra

A Shakta text that explicitly adapts ancient rites to modern conditions, affirming that sincere practice conducted with understanding rather than merely external form is available to all regardless of birth or prior initiation. The Bhairavi Chakra and Tattva Chakra described in this text are the inspiration for the Garjanaa circles described in the complete document. One of the clearest scriptural authorities for the community's approach to simplified Agamic practice.

Nyaya-Vaisheshika and Aanvikshiki Texts

Nyaya Sutras (Gautama)

The foundational text of the Nyaya school, systematizing the theory of valid knowledge (pramana), logical reasoning (anumana), the structure of valid argument, and the catalog of logical fallacies (hetvabhasa). The epistemological framework this community applies to all empirical claims. The four pramanas, perception, inference, analogy, and reliable testimony, are the basis for critical thinking in any domain.

Vaisheshika Sutras (Kanada)

The foundational text of the Vaisheshika school, providing a systematic categorization of reality through the seven padarthas: substance, quality, motion, universal, particular, inherence, and non-existence. This framework applies at the vyavaharika (empirical) level and is compatible with modern scientific categorization of natural phenomena.

Arthashastra (Kautilya) on Aanvikshiki

Describes Aanvikshiki as "the lamp of all sciences, the resource for all actions, and the support of all dharmas." The principle it establishes, that all knowledge claims in the empirical domain are subject to disciplined critical examination, is the ancestor of the scientific method and the governing standard for this community's engagement with empirical questions.

Further Texts

Yoga Sutras of Patanjali

The systematic map of Raja Yoga: the ethical foundation of Yamas and Niyamas, followed by posture, breath regulation, sense withdrawal, concentration, and meditative absorption. Patanjali's two-word definition of posture, sthira-sukham (steady and comfortable), applies equally to the whole of practice. The full treatment of Patanjali's system is in the Yoga Khanda of the complete document.

Vijnanabhairava Tantra

A Kashmir Shaiva text presenting 112 dharanas (recognition practices) through which one's nature as pure consciousness can be recognized in the midst of ordinary activities. Central to the Shambhava-upaya described in the Yoga Khanda. Many of the dharanas require no formal sitting context and are fully compatible with daily life.

A complete reading path through these texts, from most accessible to most demanding, is given in Chapter 34 of the Advaita guide and in the Charya Khanda of the complete document.
