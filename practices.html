The Living Path

Inner Toolkit

Our practices are drawn directly from the Yoga Vāsiṣṭha, Tripūrā Rahasya, and Mahānirvāṇa Tantra. They are designed to settle the body, quiet the mind, and turn attention toward its source. The formal daily sitting integrates six practices in a single arc; four more expand what that arc produces into the rest of life. Full instructions are in the Garjanaa Codes of Conduct.

The Ten Practices in Brief

1. Sūrya Nam－kāra and Āsana

Twelve postures offered as a moving prayer, each paired with one of the twelve names of the Sun. The body is settled before the breath, the breath before the mind. Six rounds of Sūrya Nam－kāra followed by whatever additional postures help you sit still without physical distraction: that capacity to sit is the measure, not athletic performance.

2. Prāṇāyāma

Breath regulation as the most direct point of leverage on the mind. Kapālabhāti clears morning dullness; Nāḍī Śodhana (alternate nostril breathing) produces the mental stillness that makes mantra and self‑inquiry accessible. The Yoga Vāsiṣṭha teaches: prāṇa and citta (mind) are the two wings of the same bird; steady one and the other follows.

3. Nyāsa: Consecrating the Body

The ritual placement of divine names on the body, turning the physical form from an object of distraction into a conscious vehicle for practice. This simple act establishes the sacred frame within which the remaining practices occur.

4. Japa and Kīrtan

Repetition of the chosen Iṣṭa mantra with a mālā, settling the mind into a single sacred current. The community mantra, Kālī‑Durge Rādheśyām, GaurīŚańkar Sītārām, gathers all five streams of approach in a single chant. Kīrtan adds collective devotion, dissolving individual self‑concern in shared voice.

5. Self‑Inquiry (Vicāra / Ātma‑anveṣaṇa)

Ask “Who am I?” as a living question, not a mantra. Trace the sense of “I” back toward its source in pure awareness. This is the central practice of Advaita, taught with precision by Ramana Maharshi and given its full philosophical grounding in the Garjanaa Advaita Guide. Every thought is traced back: to whom does this arise? To me. What is this me?

6. Chup‑Sādhana: The Discipline of Silence

At a certain point, even the effort of active inquiry can itself become restless activity. Chup‑Sādhana, as taught by Swami Ramsukhdas ji, is the direct cessation of that restlessness: simply stop. Rest in whatever quiet awareness remains when the mind’s habitual adding‑on‑to‑the‑present‑moment is set down, even briefly. This is not blankness; it is the natural stillness the path has been clearing the way toward.

7. Svādhyāya: Study of the Texts

Regular engagement with the source texts sharpens Viveka (discrimination), feeds Mananam (reflection), and supplies the understanding without which self‑inquiry lacks direction. Study is not accumulation of information. It is transformation of how one sees.

8. Community Gathering (Satsaṅga)

The company of sincere practitioners tests every quality in the presence of others. Even brief contact with genuine Satsaṅga can redirect a life; sustained exposure keeps a practitioner’s orientation alive. We gather to chant, study, sit in silence, and hold each other to the ideals the sages described.

9. Sevā: Service as Karma Yoga

Service is where non‑attachment is genuinely tested, far more honestly than in meditation. Every act of genuine giving without need for recognition or return is Karma Yoga in its actual form. The Three Da of the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upanishad: Datta (give), Damyata (restrain yourself), Dayadhvam (be compassionate). These are not contemplative ideals; they have hands and feet.

10. Ordinary Life

The point of everything above. The portraits the Gītā draws, the Sthitaprajña and the Guṇātīta, describe how a mature practitioner moves through ordinary life, not retreat conditions. Speech, relationships, work, money, and digital life are all arenas of practice. Ask “to whom does this frustration arise?” at your desk exactly as you would on the meditation cushion.

The Seven Stages of Awakening

From the Yoga Vāsiṣṭha, the seven bhūmikās describe the natural unfolding of the mind: good desire (subhecchā), self‑inquiry (vicāraṇā), thinning of the mind (tanumanāsa), attainment of equanimity (sattvapatti), non‑attachment (asaṃsakti), seeing through objects (padārthābhāvana), and the final state of Turīya: the ever‑present witness, in which even the appearance of stages dissolves. These stages are not a ladder to climb anxiously but a map for recognizing where you actually are, with honesty and without judgment.

How the Practices Fit Together

The formal sitting (Practices 1‑6) moves in a single arc: the body is settled, then the breath, then the body consecrated, then the mind given a mantra to hold, then that quieted mind turns to inquire into its own source, and finally even the inquiry is released into open silence. A complete sitting takes 60‑90 minutes; a minimum version, shortening each part without skipping any, takes 45. The remaining four practices (study, community, service, ordinary life) expand what the sitting produces into the whole of existence. None of the ten is optional; each does something the others cannot.
