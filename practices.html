Sadhana

The Path of Practice

Three streams of practice run together and sustain each other. The inner stream settles and clarifies the mind. The outer stream expresses what the inner stream reveals. The gathered stream keeps both alive through the company of sincere practitioners. None of the three is optional; each does something the others cannot.

Stream One: The Inner Arc (Morning Sadhana)

A single, unbroken arc of practice each morning, moving from the body to the breath to the sacred, then to inquiry, and finally to silence. The order matters. Each step prepares the ground for the next. A minimum sitting takes 30 minutes. A complete one takes 60 to 90. Begin with what is honestly sustainable and let it grow.

1. Body and Breath

Six rounds of Surya Namaskara offered as a moving prayer, each paired with one of the twelve names of Surya. The body is settled before the breath, the breath before the mind. Follow with whatever postures allow you to sit still without physical distraction: that ability to sit is the measure, not athletic performance. Then ten rounds of Nadi Shodhana (alternate-nostril breathing) to balance the energy channels and bring the mind to a steady, alert calm.

2. Simplified Pancha-devata Puja

A short, direct ritual of five to ten minutes, performed at a simple altar with images or symbols of the five forms. No initiation is required. No Sanskrit expertise is required. What is required is the understanding that the form is a symbol pointing to Brahman, and that the act of offering is the ego's practice of releasing its claim to the fruits of its actions.

How to set up the altar: Five images or small murtis, one for each form. A lamp (required, representing the self-luminous Atman). Incense or a flower (optional). A small vessel of water. Keep it clean and simple.

The simple daily sequence: Light the lamp. Sit quietly for one minute. Approach each of the five forms with a single mantra and a small offering (a drop of water, a flower, or simply a focused thought of gratitude). Ganesha: Om Gam Ganapataye Namah. Surya: the Gayatri Mantra. Vishnu: Om Namo Narayanaya. Shiva: Om Namah Shivaya. Devi: Om Aim Hreem Kleem. Close with a full prostration and the understanding: "I bow to Brahman in the form of this symbol." Five minutes of silence follow the puja before moving to mantra.

The principle: The Agamic tradition provides the form. Advaita Vedanta provides the understanding that the form is a pointer, not an end in itself. Together they make the ritual a genuine practice rather than a superstition or a social performance.

3. Japa and Kirtan

Repetition of the chosen Ishta mantra with a mala, settling the mind into a single sacred current. The community mantra, Kalee-Durge Radheshyam, Gaurishankar Seetaram, gathers all five streams of approach in a single chant. Kirtan, devotional singing, adds the feeling dimension, dissolving individual self-concern in shared voice. Even five minutes of sincere japa each morning produces a measurable shift in the quality of attention available for the rest of the day.

4. Self-Inquiry (Atma Vichara)

Ask "Who am I?" as a living question, not a mantra. When a thought arises, trace it: to whom does this thought occur? To me. What is this me? Turn attention toward the sense of I itself, rather than following the thought outward into its story. Hold attention at the root. When another thought arises, apply the same move again, patiently, without frustration. This practice, from Ramana Maharshi's teaching, is the central practice of Advaita. Everything else prepares the ground for it.

5. Chup-Sadhana (Silence)

At a certain point, even the effort of active inquiry can become another form of restless activity. Chup-Sadhana, the discipline of silence as taught by Swami Ramsukhdas ji, is the direct cessation of that restlessness: simply stop. Rest in whatever quiet awareness remains when the mind's habitual adding-on-to-the-present-moment is set down, even briefly, even imperfectly. The morning sitting ends here, in open, unstriving stillness, before moving into the day.

Stream Two: Seva, the Outer Sadhana

Seva is the primary external sadhana of this path. It is not supplementary charity added on to the real practice. It is the outer expression of the inner recognition: Brahman serving Brahman, through whatever nature and skill the practitioner genuinely and honestly possesses.

The world is the field. Your nature is the tool. The unconditional quality of the offering is what makes it sadhana rather than transaction. You give what you genuinely have. You expect nothing back. You dedicate the act to the Whole before beginning and release the result afterward. This is karma yoga made as concrete and personal as possible.

Seva Is Shaped by Individual Nature

What the seva looks like is determined by four factors specific to each practitioner: svabhava (one's natural character and inclination), shakti (one's genuine skill and capacity), pravritti (one's deep tendency, what one is naturally drawn to do), and ashrama (one's life-station, the actual circumstances and responsibilities of the life one is actually living). The form of seva that serves most powerfully is the one where these four factors align. Forcing a form of service that does not match one's actual nature produces neither good service nor genuine sadhana.

Examples by Life-Station

The teacher teaches freely, sharing knowledge without withholding the best of it for those who pay more. The healer treats without prejudice, bringing the full quality of care to every patient regardless of status. The artist or craftsperson creates with integrity, putting genuine quality into the work whether or not the audience is prestigious. The farmer or physical worker brings sustained care to the land and to the people the labor feeds. The engineer or builder makes things that are honest and safe rather than merely profitable. The merchant conducts trade with fairness as the non-negotiable constraint, not a PR strategy. The parent gives the fullest attention and honesty available to the formation of the next generation. The scholar or philosopher transmits what has been understood without hoarding it or inflating it. The administrator serves the people the institution was built to serve rather than the institution itself.

The medium differs; the spirit is identical in all of them. It is always: this is what I genuinely am and genuinely can do. I offer it, without condition, to the Whole. The result belongs to the Whole, not to me.

Identifying Your Seva

Three honest questions identify where your seva belongs. First: what is genuinely useful that I can do well, not what I imagine would be impressive? Second: where does my giving feel like an expression of who I am, rather than a performance of who I want to be seen as? Third: where does the need actually exist, in my immediate circle, in my community, in my field, and do my skills actually meet that need? Where these three answers converge is where the seva belongs.

Stream Three: Study and Community

Svadhyaya: Study with Aanvikshiki

Fifteen minutes of daily study from the source texts, approached with the tools of Aanvikshiki: read carefully, ask what the claim actually is, ask what evidence or reasoning supports it, ask what objections arise and whether they are well-answered. This is not skepticism used as a weapon against the tradition. It is the tradition's own insistence, through the Nyaya framework, that nothing be accepted on mere authority in the domain where reason and evidence apply. The texts can withstand scrutiny. The scrutiny itself sharpens the mind for self-inquiry.

Satsanga: Community as Practice Field

The company of sincere practitioners tests every quality in the presence of others. We gather to chant, study, sit in silence, and hold each other accountable to the ideals the sages described. Satsanga is not a social event with a spiritual overlay. It is a practice field. The honesty demanded by genuine community, the willingness to be seen clearly and to see others clearly, is one of the most reliable means of wearing down the ego's constructed self-image.

The Seven Stages from the Yoga Vasishtha

The Yoga Vasishtha describes the natural unfolding of the mind through seven bhmikas: subheccha (good desire), vicharana (inquiry), tanumanasa (thinning of the mind), sattvaapatti (attainment of clarity), asamsakti (non-attachment), padarthabhavana (seeing through the appearance of objects), and turiya (the ever-present witness in which even the appearance of stages dissolves). These are not rungs to climb anxiously. They are a map for recognizing where you actually are, with honesty and without self-congratulation or self-condemnation.
